# Trope Glossary for Indie Authors

Use this glossary to identify, name, and understand the tropes in your books. Market research note included for each major trope—knowing what readers are hungry for directly affects your positioning.

---

## Romance Tropes (30 Primary Tropes)

**Arranged Marriage**
Two characters betrothed or married without choice, often developing genuine feelings over time. Market: Evergreen. Particularly strong in contemporary romance, fantasy, and historical romance. Subgenres can position this as either coercive tension or found-family subplot.

**Grumpy/Sunshine**
One character brooding, withdrawn, or emotionally closed-off; the other bright, optimistic, or emotionally open. The contrast creates natural conflict and reconciliation. Market: High growth. Dominates contemporary romance and paranormal romance bestseller lists. Readers buy this heavily.

**Second-Chance Romance**
Reunited lovers who split, often years apart, navigating unresolved feelings and new circumstances. Market: Strong and stable. Works across all romance subgenres. Particularly popular in contemporary and paranormal.

**Forbidden Romance**
Characters attracted to each other despite social, professional, or circumstantial barriers (boss/employee, friends' exes, age gap, rival families). Market: Consistently popular. Age-gap romance has explosive growth in 2024-2026.

**Forced Proximity**
Characters trapped together—snowed in, same cabin, road trip, workplace assignment. Tension develops because they can't escape. Market: Steady demand. Works as primary plot or subplot. Often paired with grumpy/sunshine or enemies-to-lovers.

**Enemies-to-Lovers**
Characters start as antagonists and develop romantic feelings through conflict, forced collaboration, or mutual understanding. Market: Perennial bestseller. Readers never tire of this. Works across all genres and subgenres.

**One True Love**
Soulmate narrative; characters feel destined for each other. Can involve fated mates (paranormal) or simply narrative conviction that these two are each other's only option. Market: Strong in paranormal and fantasy romance. Lower in contemporary unless handled as ironic subversion.

**Love Triangle**
Three characters with overlapping romantic interests. Can be resolved or bittersweet. Market: Reader opinion polarized. Some readers love the tension; others skip books with love triangles. Know your audience.

**Fake Dating/Relationship**
Characters pretend to be together for external reasons (convince family, make someone jealous, maintain a cover) and develop real feelings. Market: High demand. Easy execution, high emotional payoff. Bestseller-generating trope.

**Found Family**
Group of non-related characters functioning as family through choice and loyalty. Often appears as subplot in romance but can be primary arc. Market: Explosive growth. Millennial and Gen Z readers prioritize found family. Pair with romance for broader appeal.

**Reverse Harem**
One character (usually female-presenting) in romantic/sexual relationship with multiple partners simultaneously, all consensual. Market: Growing niche. Requires clear communication about consent and relationship structure. Not mainstream but dedicated readership.

**Age Gap Romance**
Significant age difference between romantic partners (typically 10+ years, sometimes 20+). Market: Explosive growth 2024-2026. Often controversial but commercially strong. Pair with power dynamics considerations; transparency about character ages required.

**Childhood Friends-to-Lovers**
Characters who've known each other since youth (or in paranormal romance, have lived together lifetimes) transition to romance. Market: Steady and broad. Works in most genres. Often pairs well with forced proximity or reunion.

**Office Romance**
Characters work together and develop romantic feelings. Can include boss/subordinate dynamics (power imbalance) or peers. Market: Steady. Contemporary romance staple. Consider workplace power dynamics carefully.

**Amnesia Romance**
One character loses memory, often forgetting their relationship or the other character entirely. Creates forced reconnection. Market: Moderate. Overdone in soap operas but still appears in paranormal romance with reincarnation angles. Use sparingly.

**Bridging the Gap**
Characters from very different social backgrounds, economic classes, or worlds fall in love. Market: Steady. Works well in contemporary romance and paranormal. Often combined with forbidden romance.

**Pining**
One character secretly in love with another, often silently suffering while the object of affection is unaware or with someone else. Market: High emotional engagement. Popular in contemporary romance and paranormal. Rewards reveal-and-reunion.

**Slow Burn**
Romance develops gradually across the book (or series) with significant tension and delayed physical intimacy. Market: Growing. Readers increasingly want emotional connection before/alongside physical intimacy. Can extend across series.

**Insta-Love**
Characters fall in love rapidly, often within days or hours, moving to commitment quickly. Market: Lower in contemporary, strong in paranormal (fated mates) and fantasy. Reader tolerance depends on paranormal framework. Explain the mechanism.

**Banter**
Characters bond through witty, fast-paced verbal sparring and humor. Market: High reader satisfaction. Particularly strong in paranormal romance and romantic comedy. Readers cite banter as reason they buy repeat authors.

**Redemption Arc Romance**
One character is morally compromised or has caused harm; the other (or their love) facilitates genuine redemption. Market: Moderate to strong depending on clarity of redemption. Popular in paranormal and dark romance. Be clear about what redemption looks like.

**Power Imbalance Romance**
One character holds institutional or personal power over the other (boss, teacher, mentor, powerful creature/human dynamic). Market: Divisive. Some readers avoid; others seek it. Be explicit about consent. Often appears in paranormal romance (vampire/human, dragon/human, immortal/mortal).

**Damaged Goods Romance**
One character carries trauma, disability, or emotional/physical scars; the other's love helps them heal. Market: High emotional engagement. Be careful not to equate disability with tragedy. Authenticity matters. Growing more nuanced in quality romance.

**Secret Baby**
Hidden pregnancy or child between characters, often discovered later. Creates forced reconciliation. Market: Moderate. Contemporary romance staple. Less popular in paranormal unless tied to species reproduction logistics.

**Single Parent Romance**
One character actively parenting; romance develops alongside parenting arc. Market: Growing. Readers want realistic depiction of parenting constraints. Works across genres but requires authentic parenting representation.

**Hurt/Comfort**
One character in physical or emotional pain; the other provides care and healing. Often overlaps with hurt/comfort trope in general fiction. Market: Steady. Highly emotional. Pairs well with slow burn and second-chance romance.

**Bodyguard Romance**
One character hired (or duty-bound) to protect the other; romance develops from proximity and vulnerability. Market: Moderate to strong. Works well in paranormal and romantic suspense. Clear power dynamics necessary.

**Paranormal Species Romance**
Humans and non-humans in romantic relationships (vampire/human, shifter/human, faerie/human, etc.). Market: Strong in paranormal romance. Species crossing often tied to fated-mate narratives. Consider compatibility and power dynamics.

**Bi-awakening**
Character realizes their sexual or romantic orientation through attraction to a character of a gender they didn't expect. Market: Growing. LGBTQ+ reader market actively purchasing. Authenticity and avoiding "experimentation" framing important.

**Mortal Enemies to Lovers**
Characters not just antagonistic but actively working toward each other's destruction before falling in love. Market: Niche but dedicated. Highest emotional payoff when the shift feels earned. Popular in paranormal and fantasy romance.

**Mistaken Identity**
Characters believe someone is a different person or have false information about them. Creates complications and comedic moments. Market: Moderate. Often paired with other tropes (fake dating, forced proximity). Works better in paranormal/fantasy than contemporary (harder to sustain credibly).

**Christmas Romance**
Set during holiday season; holiday elements tie to plot and emotional resolution. Market: Seasonal but highly profitable. Readers specifically search for Christmas romance. Can be contemporary, paranormal, or fantasy.

## Fantasy Tropes (15 Primary Tropes)

**Chosen One**
Protagonist is prophesied or magically designated to defeat evil, save the world, or fulfill a destiny. Market: Foundational. Not dated if executed with complexity. Modern fantasy readers appreciate subversions where the "chosen" fights against the narrative.

**Magical School**
Characters train in magic at an educational institution (academies, covens, guilds). Market: Strong in adult fantasy. Readers love world-building through school structure. Works particularly well for paranormal romance with fantasy elements.

**Found Family in Fantasy**
Ragtag group bonded by choice, loyalty, and shared struggle. Often the emotional core. Market: Explosive growth. Readers prioritize character relationships over plot. Critical in paranormal paranormal fantasy romance.

**Dark Lord/Evil to Redeem**
Villain or morally grey antagonist gets their own book or a redemption arc. Market: High growth. Readers actively seek villain POV. Works across paranormal fantasy and dark romance.

**Portal Fantasy**
Characters transported between real world and fantasy world. Market: Steady. Creates accessibility for readers unfamiliar with high fantasy. Works in paranormal romance (human pulled into paranormal realm).

**Magic with Cost**
Magic system where using power requires payment—health, memory, years of life, sacrifice of something loved. Market: Growing. Readers appreciate logic and consequences. Differentiates your magic system from generic magic.

**Dragon/Shifter Romance**
Dragons or paranormal shapeshifters as love interests. Market: Extremely strong. One of the highest-demand paranormal romance subgenres. Readers seek dragon romance specifically.

**Fated Mates**
Paranormal/fantasy concept where two people are destined for each other through magic, species biology, or fate. Market: Dominant in paranormal romance. Reader expectation high; deliver clear mechanics for how fate works.

**Witch/Warlock Magic System**
Character whose power comes from being a practicing witch, warlock, or magical practitioner with specific rules, lineage, or covenant. Market: Steady. Works in paranormal romance, urban fantasy, and fantasy romance. Witches currently trending above warlocks.

**Vampire/Undead Romance**
Vampire or undead character as love interest. Market: Evergreen strong. Never goes out of fashion. Readers have subgenre preferences (seductive vampire vs. dark vampire vs. cursed vampire).

**Paranormal Investigation/Urban Fantasy**
Characters who investigate supernatural phenomena—detectives, paranormal investigators, hunters. Market: Strong in paranormal romance and romantic suspense. Works in series structure.

**Paranormal Apocalypse**
Fantasy or paranormal-themed end-of-world scenario. Market: Growing. Often pairs with paranormal romance as survival creates forced proximity and intimacy.

**Magic Academy Enemies-to-Lovers**
Competitors at a magical school who develop feelings. Market: High demand. Combines chosen-one/magical school with enemies-to-lovers structure. Works exceptionally well.

**Hidden Magic/Powers**
Character unaware of their own magical ability until catalyst event; discovery and control become central arc. Market: Strong foundational trope. Works in all fantasy subgenres.

**Immortal Love**
Immortal character navigates relationships with mortals or other immortals across centuries. Often tied to paranormal romance. Market: Strong. Enables hurt/comfort around mortality and legacy.

## Thriller Tropes (10 Primary Tropes)

**Unreliable Narrator**
Narrator's perspective is deliberately distorted—lying, unreliable memory, mental illness affecting perception, or deceiving the reader. Market: High critical acclaim. Readers actively seek psychological thrillers with unreliable narrators. Requires careful execution to avoid feeling gimmicky.

**Stalker/Obsessed Character**
One character fixates on another, leading to psychological or physical threat. Market: Strong in psychological thrillers. Dark romance readers seek this. Requires clear consent/non-consent framing.

**Kidnapping/Captivity**
Character taken and held against will. Can be kidnapping, hostage situation, or imprisonment. Market: Strong. Common in psychological and domestic thrillers. Requires content warning.

**Hidden Identity/Secret**
Character carries dangerous secret—about themselves, their past, or what they've done. Revelation creates turning points. Market: Universal. Works in all thriller subgenres.

**Psychological Manipulation**
One character systematically manipulates another's perception of reality (gaslighting, emotional abuse, strategic deception). Market: Strong in psychological thrillers. Often tied to abuse narratives. Requires authentic portrayal.

**Murder Mystery/Whodunit**
Character investigates a death; reader tries to solve alongside protagonist. Market: Perennial bestseller. Works as primary plot or subplot. Readers actively search for this.

**Mistaken Threat**
Character believes they're in danger from wrong source, leading to misdirected paranoia and actions. Market: Moderate. Often used for plot twist. Works well in psychological thrillers.

**Survival Against Odds**
Character must survive dangerous situation—wilderness, captivity, hostile environment, human threat. Market: Steady. Works in adventure thrillers and paranormal thrillers.

**Betrayal Twist**
Character trusted by protagonist is revealed to be antagonist or conspirator. Market: High engagement. Requires careful foreshadowing to feel earned rather than cheap.

**Serial Criminal/Killer**
Protagonist hunted by or hunting serial killer or ongoing criminal. Market: Strong. Police procedural and paranormal thriller staple. Readers seek this specifically.

## Horror Tropes (Notable Tropes for Cross-Genre)

**Haunted House/Location**
Specific place carries supernatural danger or entity. Market: Classic and durable. Works in paranormal horror and dark paranormal romance.

**Curse**
Magical consequence affecting character(s) across time or generations. Market: Strong. Works in paranormal romance and dark fantasy.

**Body Horror**
Grotesque transformation or physical deterioration. Market: Niche but intense. Popular in paranormal horror and dark paranormal romance.

**Possessed/Entity Control**
External entity controls character's body or mind. Market: Moderate. Works in paranormal horror and paranormal romance thriller.

**Creature Feature**
Non-humanoid entity as central threat—creatures, monsters, cryptids. Market: Steady in paranormal horror. Works in paranormal romance when creature is love interest.

## Genre-Crossing Notes

**Paranormal romance + thriller elements:** Combine paranormal species romance with unreliable narrator or manipulation tropes for high-tension paranormal thriller romance.

**Fantasy romance + survival:** Combine fated mates with survival against odds for paranormal paranormal fantasy romance.

**Dark romance tropes:** Redemption arcs, power imbalance, morally grey characters work in all dark romance subgenres. Clarity about consent and harm is critical.

**Series structure:** Many tropes stack effectively across books. Second-chance romance or slow burn can extend across a trilogy. Found family develops over multiple books alongside romance.

---

## How to Use This Glossary

1. Identify the tropes in your book. Most books have 3-5 primary tropes.
2. Note the market positioning for each. Strong tropes in current markets? Niche tropes? Pair niche tropes with market-strong tropes.
3. Use trope names in your book description, ads, and newsletter. Readers search tropes, not vague descriptions.
4. Don't apologize for tropes. Name them. Readers use tropes to find books they love.
