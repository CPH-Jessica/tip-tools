## SECTION 1: CHOOSING YOUR PEN NAME

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Step 1: Choose Your Pen Name"]

Your pen name is how readers find you. It needs to do three things: be discoverable, fit your genre, and not conflict with existing authors.

[PAUSE]

Here's my process:

**First, brainstorm names you actually like.** These should feel authentic to you. You're going to say this name out loud a lot—in interviews, on social media, when you introduce yourself to readers. If it makes you cringe, it's not the one.

[PAUSE]

**Second, check Amazon.** Go to the Kindle store. Search your name exactly. Look for other authors with that name in your genre and related genres. If there's already someone there, keep brainstorming. You don't want to compete with confusion.

[PAUSE]

**Third, check Goodreads.** Same process. Search the name. See if it's claimed.

[PAUSE]

**Fourth, consider discoverability.** If you choose a name like "Scarlett Rose," readers are going to find you when they search for "Scarlett Rose books" or if your name appears in keyword searches. Avoid names that are so common they're invisible, or so unusual they're unpronounceable. You want readers to remember it and be able to spell it to find you.

[PAUSE]

**Fifth, think about genre fit.** A name like "Chase Sterling" works for romantic suspense and military romance. It doesn't work for cozy mystery. "Meadow Grace" works for contemporary romance and paranormal romance. It doesn't work for dark fantasy. Your name doesn't have to be a perfect genre signal, but it shouldn't actively work against you.

[PAUSE]

I'll be honest: there are exceptions. I have a pen name that sounds soft and contemporary, but I write psychological thrillers under it. The assumption was wrong, but the books won me readers anyway. However, if you're starting out, don't make that harder on yourself. Work with genre expectations, not against them.

[PAUSE]

**Once you've chosen your name, own it.** You're going to be living with this for years. Years. Make sure you actually like it.

## SECTION 2: DEFINING YOUR GENRE AND HEAT LEVEL

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Step 2: Genre, Subgenre & Heat Level"]

Your genre tells readers what kind of book you write. Your heat level tells them what kind of content is inside.

[PAUSE]

Start with your primary genre. Are you writing romance? Contemporary fiction? Dark romance? Paranormal? The primary genre is the bucket your book lives in.

[PAUSE]

Then, your subgenre. This is where it gets specific. Within romance, are you writing paranormal romance, military romance, romantic suspense, paranormal paranormal, small-town romance? Within dark romance, are you writing dark paranormal romance or dark contemporary romance?

[PAUSE]

Most books live in at least one primary genre and one or two subgenres. Your job is to know them, because they matter for categorization on Amazon, for discoverability, and for reader expectations.

[PAUSE]

Now, the heat level. This is crucial, and I see authors get this wrong constantly.

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "The Heat Level Scale"]

We use a 1-5 scale, and here's what it means:

**Heat Level 1: Sweet/Closed Door** — No sex on the page. Kissing and emotional intimacy only. Readers expecting a Heat 1 book want the romance and the connection without sexual content. Think Hallmark movies, inspirational romance, most cozy romance.

**Heat Level 2: Warm** — Sexual content is implied or fades to black. You might fade out before sex or have a single morning-after scene, but graphic descriptions of sex acts are not here. Readers expect sensuality, not explicit content. Think mainstream contemporary romance, some paranormal romance.

**Heat Level 3: Hot** — Explicit sexual content. Multiple scenes, clear descriptions of what's happening, but it's not the focus of every chapter. This is the sweet spot for a lot of romance readers. Readers expect to be heated up, but not overwhelmed. Think most category romance, most self-published romance.

**Heat Level 4: Very Hot** — Explicit sexual content is frequent and integral to the story. Multiple detailed scenes, often throughout the book. Sexual content is part of character development or plot. Think erotic romance, some paranormal romance, some contemporary romance marketed specifically as "spicy."

**Heat Level 5: Extreme** — This goes beyond mainstream erotic romance into specific kinks, BDSM elements, or sexual content that some readers will find edgy or triggering. This is a conscious choice to serve a specific reader community, and you need to be clear about what's in your book.

[PAUSE]

Here's the thing: picking your heat level is not about what feels right to write. It's about what your target reader expects. If you're writing paranormal romance and you choose Heat Level 5, your book isn't going to reach paranormal romance readers looking for a cozy paranormal love story. You're narrowing your audience deliberately, and that's okay—but you have to know you're doing it.

[PAUSE]

I write across the heat spectrum. My contemporary romance under one pen name is Heat Level 2. My dark romance is Heat Level 4. My paranormal romance is Heat Level 3. Each choice serves the story and the reader I'm writing for.

[PAUSE]

Pick your heat level now. You can always revise it once your manuscript is done and you have a clearer sense of what you've written, but know it from the start.

## SECTION 3: YOUR SIGNATURE ELEMENTS

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Step 3: Signature Tropes & Reader Promise"]

Every author has patterns. Things that keep appearing in your books. For some authors, it's a trope. For others, it's a theme or a character archetype. Readers notice these patterns, and they come back for them.

[PAUSE]

The most common romance tropes are: fake relationship, forced proximity, enemies to lovers, fated mates, second chance romance, forbidden romance, love triangle, grumpy/sunshine, found family, reverse harem, one true pairing (OTP), broken hearts, and billionaire romance. There are hundreds more, but these are the workhorses.

[PAUSE]

Pick 3-5 tropes that genuinely excite you. These are tropes you'll return to again and again because you love writing them. Not tropes that are "in demand." Not tropes that are trending. Tropes you love.

[PAUSE]

Why? Because when readers discover your first book, they're not just buying that book. They're buying a promise about who you are as an author. If your first book is full of found family and you're naturally drawn to that theme, readers will come back for your second book expecting more found family. If your first book features a grumpy hero and that was accidental, readers won't expect it in book two.

[PAUSE]

The authors who build sustainable readerships are the ones who write from their genuine preferences. Your signature tropes aren't marketing. They're your voice.

## SECTION 4: DEFINING YOUR TARGET READER

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Step 4: Who Are You Writing For?"]

This is the question that changes everything.

[PAUSE]

You need to know who you're writing for. Not "women aged 25-45," because that's useless. I mean: What does your reader value in a romance? What's the emotional core that keeps her coming back to books?

[PAUSE]

Is she reading romance because she wants a guaranteed happy ending and escape? Is she reading because she loves complex characters in complicated situations? Is she reading for spice and heat? Is she reading for adventure and world-building with romance as the thread?

[PAUSE]

When I launched my paranormal romance pen name, my target reader was someone who loved paranormal worlds—vampires, shifters, witches—but who wanted emotional depth. She wanted a hero and heroine who earned their relationship, not just insta-love. She wanted family dynamics, loyalty, and stakes that mattered beyond just "will they or won't they." 

That clarity changed everything. I knew what she wanted in chapter one, what would make her frustrated, what would make her stay up past midnight reading.

[PAUSE]

Your target reader isn't you, necessarily. It's the reader you're trying to reach. Write down: age range (rough), life stage, what she's looking for in a book, what irritates her in books, what heat level she prefers, what she reads when she's stressed or sad.

[PAUSE]

This clarity will inform every decision moving forward.

## SECTION 5: YOUR AUTHOR VOICE & TONE

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Step 5: How You Write"]

This is different from genre. This is your voice—the actual way words come out of your brain and onto the page.

[PAUSE]

Are you witty and sarcastic? Are you lyrical and descriptive? Are you sparse and direct? Are you funny? Are you intense? Are you romantic and dreamy?

[PAUSE]

Your voice isn't something you develop after you're published. It's something you recognize and lean into. If you're naturally sarcastic, lean into sarcasm. If you're naturally lyrical, lean into lyricism.

[PAUSE]

Your target reader chose you because of your voice, not in spite of it.

[PAUSE]

Write down three words that describe how you write: the way your prose feels, the way your characters talk, the way you tell a story.

## SECTION 6: BUILDING YOUR BRAND IDENTITY

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Step 6: Visual Brand Identity"]

Your brand identity is how your pen name looks. It's your colors, your fonts, your vibe.

[PAUSE]

This matters because it creates consistency. When a reader sees your logo, your book cover, your newsletter header, she should immediately recognize it as yours. Brand consistency builds trust and recognition.

[PAUSE]

Here's what you need to decide:

**Color palette (2-3 primary colors).** Think about your genre and your reader. Dark romance? Consider deep jewel tones, blacks, rich reds. Contemporary romance? Consider softer palettes—maybe blush, cream, sage. Paranormal romance? Consider atmospheric colors—midnight blue, silver, deep purple. You're not locked into these forever, but pick a direction.

**Primary font.** You'll use this for your pen name, your logo, your header. It should be readable and reflect your vibe. Script fonts feel romantic. Sans-serif feels modern. Serif feels classic.

**Secondary font.** This is for body text, subtitles, smaller elements.

**Logo or visual mark.** This can be as simple as your initials, a symbol, or a simple mark that represents your books. You don't need to hire a designer for this—Canva has thousands of templates.

[PAUSE]

Spend 30 minutes in Canva exploring logos and brand templates for your genre. See what resonates. This doesn't have to be perfect; it just has to be yours.

## SECTION 7: YOUR AUTHOR BIO

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Step 7: Author Bio (Three Lengths)"]

You need three versions of your author bio: short, medium, and long. Different places need different lengths.

[PAUSE]

**Short bio (1-2 sentences, 30-50 words).** This goes on book covers, in Kindle book descriptions, on your Amazon author page header. Example: "Jane writes paranormal romance with grumpy heroes and found family. She lives in Portland with her partner and two cats."

**Medium bio (3-4 sentences, 75-125 words).** This goes in your newsletter footer, on your website homepage, in collaboration announcements. Example: "Jane writes paranormal romance full of slow-burn tension and family dynamics. She's a former librarian who spent her twenties reading everything and her thirties writing the paranormal romances she couldn't put down. When she's not writing, she's probably in a bookstore or planning her next trip to the Oregon coast. She lives in Portland with her partner, two rescue cats, and an embarrassing number of plants."

**Long bio (5-6 sentences, 150-250 words).** This goes in press kits, author interviews, and speaking bios. It can include your publishing journey, your inspiration, your values, or other elements that give readers deeper insight into who you are.

[PAUSE]

Write all three now. Use your real story—your actual background, your actual location, your actual life details that readers will connect with. Don't make it up. Authenticity is what builds loyalty.

[TRANSITION]

## SECTION 8: YOUR TAGLINE

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Step 8: Author Tagline"]

Your tagline is a short phrase that captures your author brand. It's not your pen name; it's the promise you're making as that author.

[PAUSE]

Examples: "Paranormal romance with bite and heart." "Grumpy heroes, found family, guaranteed happy endings." "Dark, emotional, unforgettable." "Slow-burn paranormal romance."

[PAUSE]

Your tagline should be:
- **True to your books.** It should accurately represent what you write.
- **Memorable.** Short, specific, something a reader could repeat to a friend.
- **Differentiated.** It should give readers a reason to pick you over someone else writing in your space.

[PAUSE]

Spend 15 minutes brainstorming taglines. Write down at least 5. Then pick the one that makes you feel like "yes, that's exactly who I am as this author."
