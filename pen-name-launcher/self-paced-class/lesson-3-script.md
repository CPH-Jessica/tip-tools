## SECTION 1: WHAT IS A READER MAGNET?

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "What Is a Reader Magnet?"]

A reader magnet is a free story you offer in exchange for an email address. It's not a sample of your first book. It's a complete, standalone story written in the same voice and style as your books, but shorter.

[PAUSE]

The goal is simple: to give readers a taste of your voice, your style, your tropes, and your emotional depth. They download it, they read it, and if they love it, they become subscribers. They buy your books.

[PAUSE]

This is different from a book sample. A sample is just the first few chapters of your book. A reader magnet is a new story written specifically to convert newsletter subscribers into readers.

[PAUSE]

And yes, you need one before your first book launches. Not after. Before. Because your reader magnet is how you grow your newsletter, and your newsletter is how you'll launch that first book successfully.

## SECTION 2: CHOOSING YOUR FORMAT

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Reader Magnet Format Options"]

You have three format options: short story, novella, or prequel.

[PAUSE]

**Short Story (5,000-10,000 words)** — This is quick. Readers can finish it in one sitting. The problem: it's not long enough to really showcase your voice and your emotional depth. Short stories work if you're writing in a very plot-driven genre, but they're risky for romance.

[PAUSE]

**Novella (15,000-25,000 words)** — This is my recommendation. It's long enough to deliver a complete emotional arc, multiple scenes, real stakes, and genuine heat (if that's in your brand). Readers spend 2-3 hours reading, which is enough time to fall in love with your voice and your characters. And it's still fast enough to write before your book launches.

[PAUSE]

**Prequel (20,000-30,000 words)** — This is a full story that takes place before your first book. It features characters that show up in your books. The benefit: readers finish it and immediately want to know what happens next with those characters. The drawback: you need to write your first book to know what the prequel should be about.

[PAUSE]

Go with a novella. 15,000-25,000 words. It's the sweet spot.

## SECTION 3: THE 5 CORE ELEMENTS

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "The 5 Core Elements of a Successful Reader Magnet"]

A reader magnet needs five core elements, and if you're missing one, it won't convert.

[PAUSE]

**Element 1: Protagonist**

Your reader magnet needs a main character readers care about. This isn't a broad character; this is someone with depth, history, flaws, and desires. If it's a romance, you need both a hero and a heroine with individual arcs.

[PAUSE]

Your protagonist should feel real. They should have stakes. They should want something.

[PAUSE]

**Element 2: Your Signature Tropes**

Remember Lesson 1? You picked your signature tropes. Use them here. If you're known for grumpy heroes and found family, put those in your reader magnet. Readers should finish it and think, "Oh, THIS is what she writes about. I want more."

[PAUSE]

**Element 3: Heat Level**

Make sure your reader magnet matches the heat level of your actual books. If you write Heat Level 4, your reader magnet should be Heat Level 4. If you write Heat Level 2, keep it warm but not explicit.

[PAUSE]

Why? Because readers finish your magnet and buy your book expecting the same thing. If your reader magnet is closed door but your book is spicy, you're setting up disappointment.

[PAUSE]

**Element 4: Emotional Arc**

This is the hardest part to get right. Your reader magnet needs an emotional journey. The protagonist starts at point A (afraid, guarded, broken, lonely, angry). Something happens. They grow. They transform. They end at point B (healed, loved, accepted, whole, at peace).

[PAUSE]

That emotional arc is what makes readers believe in your voice. Readers don't just read for plot. They read for transformation.

[PAUSE]

**Element 5: Reason to Want More**

By the end of your reader magnet, readers should want to read your actual books. This doesn't mean a cliffhanger. It means they should be so invested in your world and your voice that they're desperate for more.

[PAUSE]

If your reader magnet is a paranormal romance, the reader should finish it thinking, "I want to live in this paranormal world forever. I'm buying her book right now."

## SECTION 4: BUILDING YOUR STORY BIBLE

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Create Your Story Bible"]

Before you write a word, create your story bible. This is a document that outlines everything about your reader magnet world.

[PAUSE]

Your story bible should include:

**Character profiles:** For your hero and heroine (if romance), and any major supporting characters. Who are they? What do they want? What are they afraid of? What's their backstory? What makes them who they are?

[PAUSE]

**World-building:** If your reader magnet takes place in a paranormal world, a fantasy setting, a specific city, or any world different from contemporary reality, document how that world works. Rules, culture, magic system, geography—whatever is relevant.

[PAUSE]

**Plot outline:** The basic three-act structure. What's the inciting incident? What's the midpoint? What's the climax? What's the resolution?

[PAUSE]

**Tone and voice notes:** How does your protagonist talk? What's the narrative voice? Are you in first person or third? Is your prose lyrical or sparse?

[PAUSE]

**Trope notes:** Which of your signature tropes are you using here? How are they showing up in the story?

[PAUSE]

This might sound like a lot. It's not. A story bible for a novella is usually 3-5 pages. It takes an hour to create. And it saves you weeks of wandering during the writing process.

## SECTION 5: OUTLINING YOUR STORY

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Scene-by-Scene Outline"]

Next, outline your story scene by scene.

[PAUSE]

A 15,000-25,000 word novella typically has 10-15 scenes. Each scene should move the plot forward or deepen character development.

[PAUSE]

Your outline should look like this:

**Scene 1:** Hero and heroine meet. Inciting incident happens. Stakes established.

**Scene 2:** Characters deal with the inciting incident. Conflict starts. First emotional moment.

**Scene 3:** Complications. Conflict intensifies. Trust building (or breaking).

**Scene 4:** Emotional vulnerability. A moment where the characters let their guard down.

[PAUSE]

And so on.

[PAUSE]

The point of an outline is not to be rigid. It's to know where you're going before you start. You can deviate. You should deviate if the characters take you somewhere better. But you're not starting from zero.

[PAUSE]

Spend 30 minutes creating your outline. Then start writing.

## SECTION 6: WRITING YOUR READER MAGNET

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Writing: Process & Pacing"]

Here's how I write a reader magnet: I write the first draft in one week.

[PAUSE]

That's 2,000-3,000 words a day if I'm doing a 15,000 word novella. Fast. Not polished. Not perfect. Just getting the story down.

[PAUSE]

Why fast? Because if I slow down, I'll overthink it. I'll lose momentum. I'll start editing as I go, which is the death of a first draft.

[PAUSE]

You can use Claude for this. Seriously. If you have Claude Pro ($20/month), you can use it to brainstorm scenes, generate dialogue, work through plot holes, or even draft sections that are giving you trouble.

[PAUSE]

Here's how: You give Claude your story outline and scene description. Claude generates a draft. You rewrite it in your voice. You keep what works, you discard what doesn't. You've cut your writing time in half.

[PAUSE]

The first draft doesn't have to be good. It has to exist. Get it on the page. Then you edit.

## SECTION 7: EDITING YOUR READER MAGNET

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Editing: Three Passes"]

Once your draft is done, you edit in three passes.

[PAUSE]

**Pass 1: Quality Check (Big Picture)**

Read through your entire story. Does it make sense? Are there plot holes? Is the pacing off? Is the emotional arc complete? Does the ending satisfy?

[PAUSE]

This is not line editing. This is asking: Does this story work?

[PAUSE]

Fix the big stuff. Add scenes if needed. Cut scenes that don't matter. Make sure your 5 Core Elements are all present and strong.

[PAUSE]

**Pass 2: Line Edit (Sentence Level)**

Now you're looking at sentences. Is the prose clear? Are you telling when you should be showing? Are there repetitions? Is every word earning its place?

[PAUSE]

You're reading for clarity, rhythm, and voice. Does this sound like you? If you're naturally sarcastic, does the sarcasm show up? If you're naturally lyrical, are you using sensory details?

[PAUSE]

**Pass 3: Dialogue Polish (Ear Test)**

Read your dialogue out loud. Does it sound natural? Are all your characters' voices distinct? Can you tell who's speaking without dialogue tags?

[PAUSE]

This pass is just dialogue. Everything else stays as-is.

[PAUSE]

You do not need a professional editor for your reader magnet. A professional editor for a novella costs $300-800. That's useful for your first book. Your reader magnet? You can edit it yourself.

## SECTION 8: PUBLISHING YOUR READER MAGNET

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Getting Your Reader Magnet Ready"]

Once you've edited, you need to:

**Format it for delivery.** This usually means a PDF and/or an ePub file. If you're using BookFunnel or BookClicker, they have instructions on how to format and upload your file.

[PAUSE]

**Create a cover.** You can use Canva. Search "ebook cover template" and create something that looks professional and matches your brand colors. This takes 30 minutes.

[PAUSE]

**Write your metadata.** This is the description that appears when someone is about to download it. Example: "When a paranormal investigator meets a skeptical journalist, sparks fly—and so does the magic. This is the story of how two people learned to believe in impossible things. A paranormal romance novella."

[PAUSE]

**Upload to your chosen platform.** BookFunnel or BookClicker will host your reader magnet and deliver it to subscribers.

[PAUSE]

Then, in Lesson 2, you add it to Email 3 of your welcome sequence.

## SECTION 10: TIMELINE & EXPECTATIONS

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Writing Timeline"]

Here's a realistic timeline for writing a reader magnet:

**Day 1-2:** Story bible and outline (3-5 hours)
**Day 3-9:** First draft (7 days, 2,000-3,000 words/day)
**Day 10-12:** Quality check pass (rewrite big stuff)
**Day 13-16:** Line edit pass
**Day 17:** Dialogue polish
**Day 18-19:** Formatting, cover, metadata
**Day 20:** Upload and test delivery

[PAUSE]

Total: 3 weeks from brainstorm to live.

[PAUSE]

You could do this faster. You could also do it slower. The point is: this is achievable. You're not talking about months of work. You're talking about three weeks of focused effort.
