# Lesson 4: Cover Creation on a Budget
## Avatar Script — 15-20 minutes

## OPENING

[SHOW ON SCREEN: A split image — one clearly self-made Paint cover next to a professional Amazon bestseller cover]

I've seen authors with brilliant books fail because their cover screamed "I made this in Paint." Your cover is your number one sales tool. Not your blurb. Not your keywords. Your cover.

[PAUSE]

You have three seconds on a reader's screen before they scroll past. Three seconds. That thumbnail needs to communicate genre, subgenre, heat level, and quality all at once. And here's what most new authors don't realize: you don't need to spend $500 on a designer to get a cover that works.

I've launched eight pen names. My first covers? I made three of them myself for under $50 total. I'm going to walk you through exactly how I did it.

---

## WHAT COVERS COMMUNICATE

[SHOW ON SCREEN: Genre convention examples — paranormal romance, contemporary, dark romance, cozy mystery]

Your cover is a visual contract with your reader. It says: "This book is like other books you've loved."

In three seconds, your cover needs to signal:

**Genre.** A paranormal romance reader knows the aesthetic. Dark, moody, hero-focused, often with paranormal elements in the imagery. Contemporary romance is completely different. Bright, optimistic, relatable settings. Your genre readers have trained themselves to spot what they want.

[PAUSE]

**Subgenre.** Paranormal romance is not the same as paranormal mystery. Steamy contemporary is not the same as sweet contemporary. The visual language is wildly different. A cozy mystery cover screams "cozy" with warm colors and softer imagery. A psychological thriller? Dark, claustrophobic, ominous.

**Heat level.** This matters more than you think. Steamy readers want visual intensity. Kissing couples, tension, skin. Sweet romance readers want emotional connection — hands touching, intimate eye contact, vulnerability. They're different vibes entirely.

**Quality.** A reader glances at your cover and decides: "Is this book worth three dollars? Or five dollars? Or is it self-published?" Your cover sets price expectations before they read a single word. Bad covers tank rankings and reader trust. Period.

---

## THE THREE-SECOND THUMBNAIL TEST

[SHOW ON SCREEN: Amazon search result grid showing thumbnails]

This is the moment that matters. Your cover will be a thumbnail the size of a postage stamp on your reader's phone. Can they identify your genre? Can they read your title? Does it stand out from the neighboring covers?

Here's what you do: make a mockup of your cover at thumbnail size. Shrink it down. Look at it. If you can't instantly tell what genre it is, your cover doesn't work.

[PAUSE]

I tested every single one of my covers this way before publishing. One of my contemporary romance covers initially had too much negative space and got lost in a row of bold paranormal romance covers. I added more color saturation, stronger contrast, and suddenly it popped.

The three-second test isn't optional. It's market research built into your cover process.

---

## HOW TO RESEARCH COVERS IN YOUR GENRE

[SHOW ON SCREEN: Amazon category page with bestseller covers displayed]

You don't create covers in a vacuum. Your readers have trained eyes. They know what your genre looks like.

Go to Amazon. Search your exact genre and subgenre. Open the top 50 bestsellers. Look at their covers. What colors dominate? What's the typography style? Are there people on the covers or abstract imagery? Do they use photography, illustration, or graphic design?

Make a list. This isn't copying. This is speaking your reader's visual language.

I did this for each of my eight pen names. Every single time, I found patterns. For my paranormal romance, dark backgrounds dominated. For my contemporary, lighter, brighter palettes. For my dark romance, intense, moody imagery with strong character presence.

[PAUSE]

Then I looked at the thumbnails. Which covers jumped out? Why? Usually it's contrast, color boldness, and clear title text.

The Amazon browsing method takes maybe 45 minutes and saves you from hiring a designer to make something that doesn't match your genre expectations. Do this work first.

## CREATING COVERS WITH AI

[SHOW ON SCREEN: ChatGPT interface, DALL-E prompt field]

AI image generation has completely changed the indie publishing game. You can generate a cover image in ten minutes for $5 to $20 a month in credits.

**ChatGPT and DALL-E.** You pay per image generated. Start with a detailed prompt based on your genre research. You want the prompt to be specific: mood, colors, composition, character details if relevant. ChatGPT's DALL-E integration gives you four options per prompt. You can iterate.

[PAUSE]

A good prompt looks like this: "Dark paranormal romance cover. Black and deep purple background. A pale-skinned man with sharp features and silver eyes, intense expression, wearing an open black leather jacket. Atmospheric fog around him. Moody, cinematic lighting. High quality, professional fantasy art style."

Generate. Refine. Iterate. Most people get a usable image in 3-5 prompts.

Cost: roughly $0.08 per image with DALL-E credits, or included in a ChatGPT Plus subscription ($20/month).

[PAUSE]

**Bing Image Creator.** Free. It's powered by DALL-E but uses Bing's credits instead of paid tokens. You get 15 free daily creations. If you're patient and don't mind waiting, you can generate a cover image for literally nothing.

I've used Bing for several of my pen names. The quality is solid. The trade-off is slower generation and sometimes you have to wait or use the paid tier if you run through your daily credits.

Cost: completely free, or paid for faster access.

[PAUSE]

**Real talk:** AI images are good enough for indie published covers now. Not all of them. You'll generate 20 images to find one that works. But when you find the right one, you've got your hero shot.

Then you take that image into Canva and build the cover around it.

## EDITING IN CANVA

[SHOW ON SCREEN: Canva cover template, drag-and-drop interface, text tools]

Canva is where your AI image becomes a finished cover. You don't need Canva Pro for this — the free version works fine if you're disciplined about it.

**Upload your AI image.** Start with a pre-made Canva book cover template so dimensions are already correct. Drag your AI-generated image onto the template.

**Add text.** Your title and author name need to be readable at thumbnail size. Bold fonts work better than script fonts. High contrast: if your image is dark, use white or yellow text. If it's light, use dark text. Test it at thumbnail size. Always.

**Adjust composition.** You may need to move or crop your image so the title text sits on a readable background. A semi-transparent overlay can help your text pop without obscuring the image entirely.

[PAUSE]

**Fix what AI didn't.** Sometimes the AI image has awkward proportions or missing elements. Canva's tools let you adjust saturation, add accent graphics, or layer additional design elements.

I added a glowing effect to several paranormal covers, deepened shadows on dark romance covers, and added subtle geometric shapes to contemporary covers to make titles more readable.

Cost: free (with Canva's limited assets) or $15/month for Canva Pro (unlimited assets, better design elements, resize functionality).

[PAUSE]

Honest timeline: 30-60 minutes per cover in Canva if you're learning as you go. Once you've made three, you'll do the next one in 20 minutes.

## FINISHING IN BOOK BRUSH

[SHOW ON SCREEN: Book Brush dashboard, format options, final cover display]

Book Brush is your final step. It's purpose-built for indie authors and it handles all the technical requirements publishers actually care about.

**Ebook format.** Upload your cover. Book Brush ensures it meets Amazon's specifications (1600 x 2560 pixels minimum, RGB color, right DPI). It generates the exact file your KDP upload needs.

**Print format.** Same cover file, different specs. Book Brush calculates your spine width based on page count, adds bleed, adjusts for print production. You get separate files for ebook and print without starting over.

**Social graphics.** Book Brush automatically creates square social graphics from your cover — perfect for Instagram, TikTok, Facebook. One cover, multiple uses.

[PAUSE]

I've used Book Brush for every single one of my pen names. The time I save not having to resize files manually is worth the cost alone.

Cost: completely free (basic functionality) or $149/year for Plus (unlimited covers, priority support, all formats).

## WHEN TO HIRE A PROFESSIONAL DESIGNER

[SHOW ON SCREEN: Professional portfolio covers examples]

Here's when DIY doesn't make sense: if you're launching a pen name with 10+ books planned and you have budget, hire a designer. You'll have visual consistency, faster turnaround, and one less thing to manage.

Designer pricing: $50-500+ depending on complexity. A simple paranormal romance cover with AI image and custom text? $75-150. A fully illustrated fantasy romance with original art? $300-500+.

[PAUSE]

I didn't hire designers for my first three pen names. By my fourth, I had the budget and the track record to justify paying for a few covers. Now I mix and match — some I design myself for speed, some I outsource for my slower-burn pen names.

Your call depends on:
- How many books you're launching with
- Your budget
- How comfortable you are with design tools
- How quickly you need covers

Early stage? DIY. Scaling? Start outsourcing some.

## PHASE 6 RECAP

[SHOW ON SCREEN: Phase 6 workflow diagram]

Here's the whole process:

1. Research covers in your genre (45 minutes, $0)
2. Generate AI images until you find one that works (10-30 minutes, $5-20 depending on how many iterations)
3. Design in Canva (30-60 minutes first time, 20 minutes after)
4. Finish in Book Brush (10 minutes)

Total time: 2-3 hours per cover.
Total cost: $0-50 per cover if you're DIY, $75-500 if you outsource.

[PAUSE]

Your cover is the filter that separates browsers from buyers. Make it count. Make it genre-appropriate. Make it readable at thumbnail size. Then move on to publishing.
