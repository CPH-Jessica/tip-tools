## SECTION 1: WHY NEWSLETTERS ARE NON-NEGOTIABLE

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Your Email List is Your Asset"]

Here's the brutal truth: every social media platform can change overnight. Instagram can throttle your reach. TikTok can get banned. Threads can become irrelevant. Bluesky is where some people are going, but who knows if it'll last.

[PAUSE]

Your email list doesn't depend on an algorithm. It depends on your subscriber's choice to have you in their inbox. That's ownership. That's power.

[PAUSE]

When readers are on your email list, they get first access to your new releases. They get exclusive content. They see your announcements before they see them anywhere else. That's how you build a career that doesn't rely on hoping an algorithm favors you.

[PAUSE]

I have a paranormal romance pen name with 12,000 newsletter subscribers. When I launch a book, I announce it to my list first. That creates momentum that feeds into Amazon visibility, which feeds into other platforms. The newsletter is the engine.

[PAUSE]

If you're serious about a writing career, you start the newsletter now. Before your first book. Before you have a reader magnet. You start building that list immediately.

## SECTION 2: CHOOSING YOUR EMAIL PLATFORM

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Email Platform Comparison"]

You have options here. Let me walk you through the three most popular for authors and show you the actual costs.

[PAUSE]

**Mailerlite**

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Mailerlite Pricing & Features"]

Mailerlite is my recommendation for most authors starting out. Here's why: it's free up to 500 subscribers. That means you can build your entire first list, design your welcome sequence, test your newsletter—all without paying anything.

[PAUSE]

Once you hit 500 subscribers, the pricing is $10 per month. At 1,000 subscribers, it's still $10. At 2,000, it's $20. At 5,000, it's $50 per month. The pricing scales with list size, not with features.

[PAUSE]

The interface is intuitive. You can build landing pages to grow your list. You can segment subscribers. You can create automation sequences. It's built for authors, essentially.

[PAUSE]

**Kit by ConvertKit**

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Kit Pricing & Features"]

Kit is free up to 10,000 subscribers. That's huge. If you have a smaller list, Kit might never cost you anything.

[PAUSE]

Once you hit 10,000 subscribers, Kit's pricing jumps to $39 per month. Then it scales. 25,000 subscribers? $79 per month. 50,000? $129 per month.

[PAUSE]

Kit has a solid interface. You can build landing pages. You get subscriber automation. The benefit is that free tier up to 10,000.

[PAUSE]

The drawback: if you're just starting out, the free tier is so generous it actually doesn't push you to optimize your list. Some authors stay in the free tier for years and never prioritize growth the way they should.

[PAUSE]

**Mailchimp**

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Mailchimp Pricing & Features"]

Mailchimp is free up to 500 contacts. Then pricing starts at $20 per month.

[PAUSE]

The interface is less author-friendly. You can build basic campaigns, but you'll find yourself limited if you want to do anything complex.

[PAUSE]

Mailchimp is fine if you're starting a business newsletter or a general mailing list. For authors specifically, Mailerlite and Kit are better choices.

[PAUSE]

**My Recommendation**

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Platform Decision"]

If you're brand new and building your first list: **Mailerlite.** The free tier is generous, the interface is intuitive, and when you upgrade, the costs are reasonable. You're not paying for features you don't need.

[PAUSE]

If you're already established and you have 5,000+ subscribers: **Kit.** The higher subscriber tier doesn't cost you more until you hit 10,000, and you get all the features included.

[PAUSE]

Avoid Mailchimp for author work. You'll outgrow it quickly.

## SECTION 3: SETTING UP YOUR NEWSLETTER

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Setting Up: Step by Step"]

Here's what you're going to do:

**Step 1: Create your account on your chosen platform.**

This takes 10 minutes. Sign up, verify your email, choose your workspace name (this can be your pen name or your author business name).

[PAUSE]

**Step 2: Create your landing page (lead magnet gateway).**

This is the page where people sign up for your newsletter. You don't have a lead magnet yet—that's coming in Lesson 3. But you can set up a landing page that says something like: "Get exclusive release alerts and author updates from [Pen Name]. No spam, just new releases and bonus content." 

[PAUSE]

This page should have: your pen name, a brief description of what subscribers get, and an email opt-in form. Keep it simple. Keep it visual using your brand colors.

[PAUSE]

**Step 3: Set up subscriber groups (segments).**

This is important. You'll create groups like:
- Active readers (engaged, opening emails)
- New subscribers (haven't received your lead magnet yet)
- Book buyers (purchased your books)
- Free reader magnet subscribers (downloaded your freebie)

[PAUSE]

Segmentation lets you send targeted emails. You won't send the same message to someone who just signed up as you do to someone who's been with you for six months.

[PAUSE]

**Step 4: Create your newsletter template.**

This is the design of your emails. Header with your logo and pen name. Body with consistent fonts and colors from your brand identity. Footer with your social links, physical address (required by law), and unsubscribe link.

[PAUSE]

Design it once, use it forever. Your newsletter should look consistent, professional, and recognizable as yours.

## SECTION 4: YOUR WELCOME SEQUENCE (3-EMAIL SERIES)

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "The Welcome Sequence: Your First Impression"]

When someone subscribes to your newsletter, they expect to hear from you. The welcome sequence is the first three emails they receive. This is where you set expectations and build connection.

[PAUSE]

**Email 1: Welcome + Who You Are**

Subject line: something warm and direct. "Welcome to [Your Pen Name] readers!" or "You're in—here's what's next."

[PAUSE]

Body: Introduce yourself. Not a biography—a personal introduction. Why you write. What your books are about. What they're signing up for.

Example: "Hi, I'm [Pen Name], and I write paranormal romance with grumpy heroes and found family. When you subscribe to my newsletter, you get release alerts before anyone else, bonus scenes from my books, and the occasional rant about why paranormal worlds are the best worlds. No spam. Just good stuff straight to your inbox. Thanks for being here."

[PAUSE]

Email 2: Your Writing Process + Tropes

Subject line: "A bit about how I write" or "What you'll find in my books"

[PAUSE]

Body: Tell them about your writing process. What inspired you. What tropes you love (your signature tropes from Lesson 1). What readers can expect.

Example: "Most of my books feature a grumpy hero who doesn't believe in love, and a heroine who's had her own journey. I write slow-burn paranormal romance where the emotional arc matters as much as the heat level. If you love found family and second chances, you're in the right place. Here's what my readers say..."

[PAUSE]

Email 3: Your Reader Magnet (Once It Exists)

Subject line: "Something you can grab for free" or "Exclusive bonus: [Your Reader Magnet Title]"

[PAUSE]

Body: Once you've created your reader magnet (that's Lesson 3), your third welcome email introduces it. This is your gift to new subscribers. It's how you prove that you deliver value.

[PAUSE]

For now, in this lesson, placeholder: "I'm working on an exclusive gift for my readers. In the meantime, [link to your first book on Amazon, or a landing page coming soon]."

## SECTION 5: NEWSLETTER SCHEDULE & SENDING

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "The Newsletter Cadence"]

How often should you send your newsletter?

[PAUSE]

The answer is: whatever schedule you can sustain.

[PAUSE]

Some authors send weekly. Some send monthly. Some send irregular (whenever they have an announcement).

[PAUSE]

Here's what matters: consistency. If you say you send weekly, you send weekly. If you say monthly, you send monthly. Your subscribers signed up for a rhythm. Respect it.

[PAUSE]

My paranormal romance pen name sends monthly. That's 12 emails a year. It's sustainable, it's enough to stay in readers' minds, and it's not so frequent that people unsubscribe.

[PAUSE]

My contemporary romance pen name sends bi-weekly. That's 24 emails a year.

[PAUSE]

My dark romance pen name sends weekly during launch season and monthly otherwise.

[PAUSE]

Pick your cadence based on:
- How much time you have to write emails
- How much you have to say
- What feels sustainable for years, not weeks

[PAUSE]

Consistency beats frequency. A monthly email you actually send beats a weekly email you can't keep up with.

## SECTION 6: NEWSLETTER SWAPS (YOUR GROWTH ENGINE)

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Newsletter Swaps: The #1 Growth Tool"]

Okay. This is the game-changer.

[PAUSE]

A newsletter swap is when you and another author with a similar subscriber base cross-promote each other. You send an email to your subscribers introducing their book and newsletter, and they do the same for you.

[PAUSE]

Why does this work? Because you're reaching readers who already like books in your genre, recommended by someone they trust (another author). That's the highest-quality lead you can get.

[PAUSE]

When I launched my paranormal romance pen name, I didn't have a huge list. But I did swaps with authors who had 2,000-5,000 subscribers. Each swap brought me 50-200 new subscribers. After 20 swaps over six months, I had a list of 5,000. Now I have 12,000.

[PAUSE]

**How to Do a Newsletter Swap**

Step 1: Find an author writing in your genre with a similar or slightly larger list. Not a massive author—those swaps don't work well. You want someone roughly your size.

Step 2: Send them a DM or email: "I love your work. Would you be interested in a newsletter swap? I have 500 subscribers interested in paranormal romance."

Step 3: If they agree, you both create a short promotion (3-5 sentences) for each other's newsletter. You send it to your list. They send yours to theirs.

Step 4: That's it. You've just reached 500+ new potential readers.

[PAUSE]

The platforms that make swaps easy:

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Newsletter Swap Platforms"]

**BookFunnel** — Cost: $30 to $300 per year (depending on your tier). BookFunnel is primarily a promotional platform, but they have a newsletter feature. You can set up a landing page where your book or lead magnet is delivered. Then you use that landing page in your swaps. Writers love BookFunnel because it's built for authors. 

[PAUSE]

**BookClicker** — Cost: Free. BookClicker is a free platform where you can set up your lead magnet and create a landing page. It's not as polished as BookFunnel, but it's free, and it works.

[PAUSE]

**Bookswappy** — Cost: Free. Bookswappy is specifically designed for newsletter swaps. You create your profile, you list what you're looking for in a swap partner, and you can browse other authors' profiles. It's a marketplace for swaps.

[PAUSE]

Most authors use a combination. BookFunnel or BookClicker for the landing page, and then Bookswappy to find swap partners.

[PAUSE]

**What Not to Do in Swaps**

Don't swap with someone outside your genre. A paranormal romance author swapping with a mystery author means the mystery author's subscribers see paranormal romance. Mismatch. Bad conversions.

Don't do a swap just because someone asked. Do swaps with authors you actually like and respect. Your reputation is on the line.

Don't expect massive conversions right away. A good swap converts 10-15% of email opens. If they send your promo to 1,000 people and get 30% open rate, that's 300 opens and 30-45 new subscribers. That's solid.

## SECTION 7: YOUR SWAP PARTNER STRATEGY

[SHOW ON SCREEN: "Building Your Swap Network"]

Your goal for the next year: do 20-30 swaps.

[PAUSE]

Start with authors you already follow or know. Reach out personally. "I love your paranormal romance. I'm launching my pen name and I'm doing swaps to build my list. Would you swap with me?"

[PAUSE]

Keep a spreadsheet: Author name, genre, list size, date of swap, new subscribers gained. This helps you track what's working.

[PAUSE]

After you've done 5-10 swaps, you'll notice patterns. Certain authors' audiences convert better than others. Certain genres cross-pollinate better.

[PAUSE]

Use those patterns. Keep swapping with what works.

[PAUSE]

The authors with the fastest growing lists aren't the ones posting on social media all day. They're the ones doing consistent newsletter swaps.
