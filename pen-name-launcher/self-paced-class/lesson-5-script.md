## WHAT KDP OFFERS

[SHOW ON SCREEN: KDP account dashboard showing ebook, paperback, hardcover options]

KDP stands for Kindle Direct Publishing. It's Amazon's platform for indie authors. When you publish on KDP, you're not going through a publishing house. You control everything: pricing, description, categories, keywords, launch date.

KDP lets you publish:

**Ebook** — The Kindle format. Your reader buys it on Amazon, downloads it to their Kindle device or app, and reads. This is where most of your sales will come from.

[PAUSE]

**Paperback** — Print-on-demand. You set a price, KDP covers printing costs, you keep the royalty. No inventory. No upfront printing. They print one copy at a time when someone orders.

**Hardcover** — Same as paperback but hardcover format. Fewer sales typically, but higher price point means better royalties per copy.

Most indie authors start with ebook, add paperback after they know the book sells, and maybe add hardcover later.

[PAUSE]

I published my first book ebook-only. My second, I added paperback. My fourth, I added hardcover for a specific pen name. You don't need all three on day one.

## THE BIG QUESTION: KINDLE UNLIMITED

[SHOW ON SCREEN: Kindle Unlimited logo and explanation graphic]

This is the fork in the road. Every book you publish on KDP, you have to choose: Kindle Unlimited or wide distribution.

Let me be crystal clear about what each one means.

**Kindle Unlimited (KU):**
Your book is exclusive to Amazon. No Apple Books. No Kobo. No B&N. No libraries. ONLY Kindle. In exchange, Amazon pays you from their Kindle Unlimited pot — their monthly subscription service where readers pay a flat fee and can read unlimited books.

[PAUSE]

**How KU pays:**
Amazon puts millions of dollars into a pool every month. They pay authors based on pages read. Let's say the KENP fund is $50 million that month. You wrote a 300-page book and readers read 150 million pages total that month. If 150,000 pages of your book were read, you get: (150,000 / 150,000,000) × $50,000,000 = $50,000.

[PAUSE]

That math is made up. Real numbers vary wildly. But the point is: you earn from pages read, not copies sold.

[PAUSE]

**Wide Distribution:**
Your book is on Amazon, Apple Books, Kobo, Barnes & Noble, Google Play, libraries, everywhere. Readers pay per copy. You keep the royalty (usually 35-70% depending on price). No exclusivity requirement.

---

## KINDLE UNLIMITED: PROS & CONS

[SHOW ON SCREEN: Two-column comparison]

**KU PROS:**
- Amazon's algorithm favors KU books (more visibility)
- KENP page reads can be substantial if your book is addictive
- Easier to run KU-exclusive promotions
- Readers more likely to finish KU books (already paid, less guilt about "wasting money")
- Good for series (readers binge, you get paid for all pages)

[PAUSE]

**KU CONS:**
- Exclusive to Amazon (no Apple, no libraries, no Kobo)
- Earnings depend on Amazon's pool size (can fluctuate 20-40% month to month)
- Must stay in KU for 90 days minimum (can't test wide, come back)
- If you're already successful elsewhere (newsletters, TikTok), you're locked out of those readers
- No control over price (Amazon sets it between $0.99-9.99 for maximum royalties)

---

## WIDE DISTRIBUTION PROS & CONS

[SHOW ON SCREEN: Two-column comparison with distribution map]

**WIDE PROS:**
- Available on every major platform (reach readers wherever they are)
- You control pricing (can charge $3.99 or $7.99)
- Can give away books free for marketing (boosted by free promotions)
- Libraries stock wide books (passive income)
- Better for established platforms (newsletters, TikTok drives Apple/Kobo sales)
- No exclusivity lock-in

[PAUSE]

**WIDE CONS:**
- Amazon doesn't promote wide books as aggressively
- No access to KENP (page read) earnings
- Harder to run mass promotions (each platform handles promo differently)
- Slower discoverability on Amazon (KU books rank higher)
- Audiobook deals harder to negotiate if not on KU first

## THE HONEST KU VS WIDE DECISION

[SHOW ON SCREEN: Decision matrix with scenarios]

Here's how I think about it for each pen name:

**Go KU if:**
- This is your first book in a brand new pen name
- You're planning to write a series (KU rewards series readers)
- You have no existing audience outside Amazon
- You want to test KU payouts (lock in for 90 days minimum)
- You're focused on organic Amazon sales

**Go Wide if:**
- You have an established newsletter outside Amazon
- You're using TikTok/Bookstagram to drive sales (those audiences buy everywhere)
- You want to maximize total distribution
- You've already tested KU and it wasn't working
- You want to submit to libraries and BookBub

[PAUSE]

I've done both. My most successful pen name is KU (paranormal romance series). My second most successful is wide (contemporary romance, strong TikTok following). Both work. The decision is about your specific situation.

[PAUSE]

Real talk: don't agonize over this. You can always go wide after your KU 90-day commitment ends. Try KU first, see what the KENP payout looks like, then decide. Most new authors do better starting KU because Amazon's algorithm boost matters when you're starting from zero.

## CATEGORIES AND KEYWORDS

[SHOW ON SCREEN: KDP category selection interface and keyword input field]

After you decide KU vs wide, you need to choose categories and keywords. These determine who discovers your book.

**Categories:**
KDP gives you two category slots. Pick the most specific categories available. "Romance" is too broad. "Paranormal Romance" is better. "Paranormal Romance > Vampires" is best.

Go to Amazon, search your genre, find successful books, look at their category breadcrumbs. That's your template.

[PAUSE]

**Keywords:**
Seven keyword slots. Use all of them. These are search terms. "Paranormal romance," "vampire romance," "paranormal fantasy," "paranormal series," etc.

You have two options: manual research or Publisher Rocket.

[PAUSE]

**Publisher Rocket** ($199 one-time purchase) analyzes Amazon's search data and tells you which keywords get searched most frequently and have the least competition. Worth it if you're serious. I use it for every book now.

[PAUSE]

**Manual method** (free):
Go to Amazon search bar. Type your category. Look at the auto-complete suggestions. Those are real searches people are doing. Pick the ones with 500-5000 monthly searches. If it has fewer than 500 searches, it's too niche. If it's over 10,000, competition is fierce.

[PAUSE]

I did this manually for my first three books. By book four, I bought Publisher Rocket and saved hours per book. It paid for itself.

## PRICING STRATEGY

[SHOW ON SCREEN: Price point chart showing royalty tiers]

Here's the math that matters.

**KDP Royalty Tiers:**
- $0.99-2.98: 35% royalty
- $2.99-9.99: 70% royalty
- $10+: 35% royalty

[PAUSE]

The magic sweet spot for indie romance is $2.99 for ebook. At 70% royalty, a $2.99 book earns you $2.09 per sale. A $4.99 book earns you $3.49 per sale. But most readers will buy the $2.99 book. You sell way more copies.

Volume beats price point when you're starting out.

[PAUSE]

**Launch Strategy:**
Many authors launch at $0.99 for a week (visibility boost), then move to $2.99 permanent price. Some launch straight at $2.99. Some run promotions at $0.99 monthly.

My pattern: launch at $2.99, run $0.99 promotions every few months to boost ranking, then reset to $2.99.

[PAUSE]

**Paperback Pricing:**
Don't cheap out on paperbacks. Set a price that covers production cost (usually $6-8 for a 300-page romance) plus reasonable royalty. KDP suggests print prices. Take it or beat it slightly.

**Hardcover Pricing:**
Premium price. $17.99-24.99 typical. Hardcover readers are willing to pay more. Smaller sales volume but better royalty per copy.

## WHEN TO GO KU, WHEN TO GO WIDE

[SHOW ON SCREEN: Decision flowchart]

Here's my framework:

**Book 1 in new pen name?** Start KU (90-day trial). See what KENP pays. You have zero external audience anyway.

**Book 2 in same pen name?** Stay KU (series readers trust the exclusivity). Build momentum. See if KENP is sustainable.

**Book 3+?** If KENP is paying well, stay KU. If it's not, go wide. If you have strong newsletter/social, go wide and drive external traffic.

**Established author with existing audience?** Go wide immediately. Your readers are already on other platforms.
