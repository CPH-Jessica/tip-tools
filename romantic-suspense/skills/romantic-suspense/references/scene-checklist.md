# Romantic Suspense Scene Checklist -- Detailed Guide

Use this checklist to verify every scene in your romantic suspense manuscript before considering it complete. Each item includes an explanation of WHY it matters and HOW to fix it if it is missing.

---

## 1. Both Engines Present

**Check:** Does this scene advance the romance arc, the suspense arc, or (ideally) both?

**Why it matters:** This is the defining requirement of the genre. A romantic suspense scene that only advances one arc is acceptable occasionally, but scenes that advance neither are dead weight. The best scenes advance both simultaneously -- the investigation forces emotional vulnerability, or the romantic tension complicates a tactical decision.

**How to fix:** Identify which arc the scene primarily serves. Then add at least one beat from the other arc. In a suspense scene: a glance, a protective gesture, a thought about the other person. In a romance scene: a reminder of the danger, a locked door, a mention of the case.

**Red flag:** Three or more consecutive scenes that only serve one arc. The other engine has stalled.

---

## 2. POV Is Clear

**Check:** Does the reader know whose head they are in within the first paragraph?

**Why it matters:** Romantic suspense uses dual POV, which means the reader needs an immediate signal at the start of each scene. Confusion about POV breaks immersion and muddies both the emotional and suspense arcs.

**How to fix:** Name the POV character or use a distinctive internal thought within the first 2-3 sentences. Avoid opening with dialogue from a non-POV character. Some authors use the character's name as a scene or chapter header -- this is a clean solution.

**Red flag:** The reader has to read half a page before knowing whose scene this is.

---

## 3. Physical Grounding

**Check:** Are at least 2-3 senses present? Is the reader physically IN the scene?

**Why it matters:** Both engines need sensory grounding. The suspense needs the reader to feel the danger (the cold of a gun barrel, the smell of smoke, the sound of footsteps in an empty hallway). The romance needs the reader to feel the attraction (the warmth of a hand, a scent that is distinctly his, the rough texture of a jaw). Ungrounded scenes float.

**How to fix:** Add non-visual sensory details. Focus on what the POV character would notice: sounds, smells, textures, temperature, taste. Choose details that serve the active engine -- leather and gunpowder for suspense, skin and warmth for romance.

**Red flag:** A scene that reads like a screenplay -- all dialogue and visual description with no other sensory input.

---

## 4. Tension Present

**Check:** Is there romantic tension, suspense tension, or both in this scene?

**Why it matters:** Romantic suspense should never have a neutral scene. Every scene should make the reader feel something -- anticipation, dread, desire, fear, hope. If a scene has no tension of any kind, the reader's engagement drops and may not recover.

**How to fix:** Identify the tension source. Options: unresolved romantic feelings, physical danger, secrets being kept, a ticking clock, professional conflict, a power dynamic, sexual tension, distrust. If none exists, the scene needs a complication added or needs to be cut.

**Red flag:** A scene where everyone is comfortable, nothing is at stake, and the reader could skip it without losing anything.

---

## 5. Something at Stake

**Check:** What does the POV character stand to gain or lose in this scene?

**Why it matters:** Stakes are what make the reader care. In romantic suspense, stakes come in pairs: the physical stakes (someone might die) and the emotional stakes (someone might get hurt). The best scenes put both at risk.

**How to fix:** Name the stakes before writing the scene. Physical: life, safety, evidence, a suspect. Emotional: trust, vulnerability, the relationship, self-respect. Make sure the reader can feel what is at risk.

---

## 6. Competence on Display

**Check:** Does at least one character demonstrate skill, intelligence, or capability in this scene?

**Why it matters:** Romantic suspense readers love competent characters. Watching someone be excellent at their job -- picking a lock, reading a crime scene, defusing a bomb, hacking a database, talking down a suspect -- is both thrilling and attractive. Competence drives BOTH engines.

**How to fix:** Give at least one character a moment of expertise. It does not need to be dramatic -- noticing a detail others missed, making a quick tactical decision, knowing exactly the right thing to say in a crisis. The other character should notice and be affected.

**Red flag:** Characters who are supposedly experts but never demonstrate their expertise on page.

---

## 7. Vulnerability Present

**Check:** Does at least one character show or deliberately hide a vulnerability?

**Why it matters:** Competence without vulnerability creates flat characters. The moment a tough, capable character shows their soft spot -- their fear, their wound, their weakness -- the reader falls for them. In romantic suspense, vulnerability is both the romantic engine (they let their guard down for this person) and a suspense tool (the villain can exploit what they care about).

**How to fix:** Choose one moment in the scene where a character's armor cracks. It can be shown (a flinch, a catch in the voice, a hand that trembles) or hidden (the character overcompensates, deflects with humor, changes the subject). Both are effective.

---

## 8. Dialogue Does Work

**Check:** Does every line of dialogue advance plot, character, or relationship?

**Why it matters:** Romantic suspense is dialogue-heavy. Characters interrogate suspects, argue about tactics, confess feelings, lie to each other, and say the quiet thing loud. Every line needs to earn its place. Filler dialogue ("How are you?" "Fine.") kills pace.

**How to fix:** For each exchange, ask: what does each character WANT from this conversation? What are they hiding? What is the subtext? If a line does not serve any of these, cut it. Double-duty dialogue (advancing plot AND revealing character) is the goal.

---

## 9. The Threat Is Remembered

**Check:** Even in a romance-focused scene, is the active danger acknowledged?

**Why it matters:** The threat is the ticking clock that gives the romance its urgency. If characters are having a tender moment but the reader has forgotten someone wants to kill them, the scene loses its genre identity. The danger does not need to be front and center in every romance scene, but it should be audible in the background.

**How to fix:** Add one beat: a locked door, a glance at a phone, a mention of the case, a sound outside, a weapon within reach. The characters can be in a moment of intimacy AND aware that the world outside is dangerous. This combination IS the genre.

---

## 10. The Attraction Is Felt

**Check:** Even in a suspense-focused scene, is the romantic awareness present?

**Why it matters:** The flip side of rule 9. During an investigation, a chase, or a confrontation, the leads should still be aware of each other as attractive people. A glance at her mouth during a briefing. The way his competence makes her pulse jump for reasons that have nothing to do with danger. This is what makes it romantic suspense instead of just suspense.

**How to fix:** Add one beat of physical or emotional awareness. The POV character notices something about the other person -- their hands, their focus, the way they move, their scent. It should be quick (a line or two) but present.

---

## 11. Hook at End

**Check:** Does the scene end on a question, cliffhanger, revelation, or charged moment that makes the reader turn the page?

**Why it matters:** Romantic suspense is a page-turner genre. Every scene ending should create momentum. The reader should not be able to comfortably stop.

**How to fix:** End on: a threat revealed, a kiss interrupted, a clue discovered, a confession started, a figure in the dark, a phone call, a revelation that changes everything. Cut before the resolution. Cut before the character processes what just happened. End on the impact, not the reaction.

**Red flag:** A scene that ends with the character reflecting on what happened or going to sleep.

---

## 12. Pacing Matches Content

**Check:** Do sentence length, paragraph length, and scene rhythm match the emotional and narrative content?

**Why it matters:** Romantic suspense requires a voice that can shift gears. Action scenes need short, punchy sentences and rapid-fire paragraphs. Intimate scenes need longer, flowing prose and room to breathe. Getting this wrong creates tonal whiplash.

**How to fix:** Read the scene aloud. Action sequences should feel breathless. Romantic sequences should feel warm and unhurried. The transition between registers should be smooth -- not a hard cut from machine-gun prose to purple romance.

---

## 13. No Info Dumps

**Check:** Is procedural, technical, or backstory information woven into the scene rather than delivered in blocks?

**Why it matters:** Romantic suspense involves law enforcement procedures, forensic details, military operations, or technical knowledge. This information is necessary but deadly when dumped in paragraphs. The reader wants to feel smart, not lectured.

**How to fix:** Deliver information through action and dialogue. Show the character processing a crime scene while talking to their partner. Reveal backstory through argument, not flashback. If a technical detail is necessary, keep it to 2-3 sentences maximum before returning to action or character.

**Red flag:** A paragraph or more of unbroken procedural or technical detail with no character reaction or action breaking it up.

---

## 14. The Scene Earns Its Place

**Check:** If you removed this scene, would the book be weaker?

**Why it matters:** Romantic suspense cannot afford filler. The dual-engine structure means every scene needs to advance at least one arc, ideally both. A scene that exists only to move characters from point A to point B should be summarized in a paragraph or given a complication that makes it essential.

**How to fix:** If the scene does not earn its place, either add a complication (suspense), a character revelation (romance), or cut it and summarize the transition.

---

## Quick-Reference Scoring

When evaluating a scene, score each item:
- **PASS** -- The element is present and effective
- **NEEDS WORK** -- The element is present but underdeveloped
- **MISSING** -- The element is absent

A strong romantic suspense scene should have 11+ items at PASS. Items 1 (both engines) and 9-10 (threat remembered / attraction felt) are the genre-defining requirements -- these should always be PASS. Other items can be NEEDS WORK in a first draft.

### The Dual-Engine Quick Test:
After scoring, check: did the scene advance the suspense arc? Did it advance the romance arc? If it advanced both, that is an A+ scene. If it advanced one, acceptable. If it advanced neither, the scene must be revised or cut.
