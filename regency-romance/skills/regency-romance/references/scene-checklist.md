# Regency Romance Scene Checklist -- Detailed Guide

Use this checklist to verify every scene in your Regency romance before considering it complete. Each item includes an explanation of WHY it matters and HOW to fix it if it is missing.

---

## 1. No Contractions

**Check:** Are there ANY contractions in dialogue or narration?

**Why it matters:** Contractions are the single fastest way to shatter the Regency voice. One "don't" in a duke's dialogue pulls the reader out of 1815 and into the present. This is non-negotiable.

**How to fix:** Do a search for all common contractions: n't, 'm, 're, 've, 'll, 's (as contraction), let's. Replace every one. "Do not" for "don't." "I am" for "I'm." "Shall not" for "won't."

**Exception:** Lower-class characters in dialogue may occasionally use contractions to mark social class. This should be a deliberate choice, not an oversight.

---

## 2. Period-Appropriate Word Choices

**Check:** Are there any modern words, phrases, or expressions that break the period?

**Why it matters:** A single anachronistic word can destroy pages of careful atmosphere. "Okay" in a Regency drawing room is a time machine the reader did not ask to board.

**How to fix:** Scan for common offenders: okay/OK, hello, goodbye, nice (meaning good), bored, interesting (as vague positive), contact (as verb), reliable. Refer to the period language guide for substitutions. When in doubt, ask: would Jane Austen have written this word in this context?

**Red flag:** If a line of dialogue could be said unchanged in a modern coffee shop, it needs work.

---

## 3. Correct Forms of Address

**Check:** Is every character addressed correctly for their rank, throughout the entire scene?

**Why it matters:** Getting titles wrong is one of the most commonly noted errors by Regency romance readers. A duke addressed as "Lord" instead of "Your Grace" signals to knowledgeable readers that the author has not done their research. This erodes trust in everything else.

**How to fix:** Refer to the forms of address table in the main SKILL.md. Key rules:
- Dukes are "Your Grace," never "My Lord"
- Earls, viscounts, and barons are "My Lord" or "Lord [Place Name]"
- Baronets are "Sir [First Name]"
- Unmarried women are "Miss [Surname]" (eldest daughter) or "Miss [First Name] [Surname]" (younger daughters)
- The shift from formal to first-name address IS a plot point. Use it deliberately.

---

## 4. Social Rules Observed (or Deliberately Broken)

**Check:** Are the social rules of the period being followed? If broken, are there consequences?

**Why it matters:** The social rules ARE the story engine. A Regency romance without social consequences is just a contemporary romance in costume. When characters break rules (being alone together, dancing three times, using first names), it must MEAN something and cost something.

**How to fix:** For every interaction, ask: would this be permitted? If a man and woman are alone together, someone should note the impropriety -- either they are conscious of the risk, or a chaperone is present, or the circumstances are explained. If rules are broken, the narrative must acknowledge the stakes.

**Red flag:** Characters behaving with modern freedom (texting-era casual intimacy) in a Regency setting with no one blinking.

---

## 5. Physical Behavior Appropriate to Period

**Check:** Is physical contact between characters appropriate to their relationship stage and the period's rules?

**Why it matters:** In the Regency, physical contact was loaded. A hand touched through a glove could carry the weight of a modern love scene. If characters are casually touching, embracing, or kissing before engagement, the stakes of the romance collapse.

**How to fix:** Map the physical contact to the courtship stage:
- Pre-introduction: No contact
- Early acquaintance: Hand-kissing (formal), assisted into carriage (impersonal but electric)
- Growing attraction: Accidental touches, brushed fingers, a hand on an arm
- Pre-declaration: Near-kisses, almost-touches, charged proximity
- Post-declaration/engagement: First kiss, hand-holding in private, restrained intimacy

Each escalation should feel enormous because the rules make it enormous.

---

## 6. Romantic Tension Present

**Check:** Even in scenes that are not primarily romantic, is the awareness between the leads still simmering?

**Why it matters:** This is a romance. The romantic tension should be the through-line. Even in a scene about estate business or a family dinner, if both leads are present, they should be aware of each other. A glance, a thought, a physical reaction.

**How to fix:** Add one beat of romantic awareness per scene where both leads are present. It can be subtle: noticing how the candlelight catches their hair, being distracted during a conversation, feeling their proximity in a crowded room.

**Red flag:** Both leads are in the same room for an entire scene and neither one thinks about the other.

---

## 7. Wit and Verbal Sparring

**Check:** Is there verbal wit, social comedy, or sparring present where appropriate?

**Why it matters:** Wit is the Regency romance's signature flavor. It is what separates the genre from generic historical romance. Verbal sparring shows compatibility, intelligence, and the kind of intimacy that comes from truly seeing another person. Austen's couples fall in love through conversation.

**How to fix:** Give characters opinions and the language to express them. Let them disagree articulately. Use social situations for comedy of manners. The heroine should observe the absurdity of her world with intelligence; the hero should be surprised and intrigued by her mind.

**Red flag:** Characters who are described as witty but whose actual dialogue is flat.

---

## 8. Heat Comes from Restraint

**Check:** Does the romantic tension derive from what characters WANT but CANNOT do, rather than what they actually do?

**Why it matters:** This is the fundamental engine of Regency romance heat. In a world where a bare hand is scandalous, the restraint itself is erotic. The longing glance across a ballroom, the almost-touch, the things they say with their eyes that they cannot say with their mouths -- this is where the genre's particular magic lives.

**How to fix:** For every moment of physical awareness, ask: what do they want to do that they cannot? Show the wanting. Show the restraint. Show the cost of propriety. The space between desire and permission is where the heat lives.

---

## 9. Sensory Details Ground the Reader

**Check:** Is the reader grounded in the physical world of the scene through period-appropriate sensory details?

**Why it matters:** The Regency world should feel lived-in: the weight of silk, the warmth of candlelight, the sound of a pianoforte, the scent of beeswax candles and hothouse flowers. These details transport the reader and make the setting real.

**How to fix:** Include at least 2-3 sensory details per scene that are specific to the period. Focus on: fabrics and textures (muslin, silk, wool), lighting (candles, firelight), sounds (carriages, music, conversation), smells (flowers, food, smoke, perfume), and physical sensations (the heat of a ballroom, the chill of a garden, the weight of formal clothing).

**Red flag:** A scene that could take place in any era because the sensory details are generic.

---

## 10. Setting Is Period-Accurate

**Check:** Are furniture, food, transportation, clothing, and domestic details appropriate to the Regency period?

**Why it matters:** Material details build the world. Getting them wrong -- mentioning gas lights in 1812, having a character eat a banana, describing a zipper -- breaks the reader's immersion.

**How to fix:** Cross-reference any material detail you are unsure of. Common areas to check: lighting (candles and oil lamps, not gas in early Regency), clothing (Empire waist, stays not corsets in the modern sense, no zippers or elastic), food (what was available and common), and transportation (horses and carriages, not trains).

---

## 11. Longer Sentence Structures

**Check:** Does the prose use complex, period-appropriate sentence structures?

**Why it matters:** Short, punchy modern sentences break the Regency voice as surely as contractions do. The period prose style uses dependent clauses, inversions, and longer constructions that create a particular rhythm. This does not mean EVERY sentence should be long -- variety is essential -- but the overall texture should feel more formal than modern commercial fiction.

**How to fix:** Look for clusters of short, simple sentences and combine some into more complex structures. Use inversions for emphasis. Add dependent clauses. But maintain readability -- if you have to read a sentence three times to parse it, it is too complex.

---

## 12. Feelings Expressed Indirectly

**Check:** Are characters' strongest feelings expressed through behavior, subtext, and understatement rather than direct declaration?

**Why it matters:** Regency characters (especially well-bred ones) do not say "I love you" casually. They speak of "regard," "esteem," "affection," "attachment." They show their feelings through actions: attending to someone's comfort, seeking their company, defending their reputation. The indirect expression is what makes the eventual direct confession so powerful.

**How to fix:** Replace direct emotional statements with indirect ones. Instead of "He loved her," try "He found that no room was quite comfortable without her in it." Instead of "She was attracted to him," try "She was excessively aware of the space between them."

---

## 13. Scene Opens in Action

**Check:** Does the scene begin in the middle of something happening?

**Why it matters:** Opening with weather, backstory, or a character reflecting on their situation kills momentum. Start in the middle of a conversation, a social event, an arrival, a decision -- something that puts the reader into motion immediately.

**How to fix:** Cut everything before the first moment of action or dialogue. If the scene opens with three paragraphs of description before anything happens, start with the thing that happens and weave the description around it.

---

## 14. Scene Ends on a Hook

**Check:** Does the scene end on a question, a glance, a revelation, or a moment that pulls the reader forward?

**Why it matters:** Every scene ending is either a door that closes (the reader stops) or a hand that pulls (the reader continues). In romance, the hook can be romantic (an interrupted moment, a loaded look, an overheard confession) or plot-driven (a letter arrives, a secret is revealed, an obstacle appears).

**How to fix:** Cut the last paragraph. Does the scene still work? Often the real ending is buried one paragraph before where you stopped writing. End on the image, the line, the moment -- not the reflection about the moment.

---

## 15. Single POV Maintained

**Check:** Does the scene stay in one character's point of view throughout?

**Why it matters:** Head-hopping -- shifting between characters' thoughts within a scene -- is disorienting and dilutes both the romantic tension and the reader's connection to the POV character. Single POV per scene is the standard for modern romance.

**How to fix:** Pick the character with the most at stake in the scene and stay in their head. Show the other character's feelings through observable behavior: expressions, gestures, tone of voice, word choices. The reader should be interpreting the other character alongside the POV character.

---

## Quick-Reference Scoring

When evaluating a scene, score each item:
- **PASS** -- The element is present and effective
- **NEEDS WORK** -- The element is present but underdeveloped
- **MISSING** -- The element is absent

A strong Regency romance scene should have 12+ items at PASS. Language items (1-3) are non-negotiable -- these should always be PASS. Other items can be NEEDS WORK in a first draft without blocking forward progress.
