# Regency Period Language Guide

A comprehensive reference for writing period-accurate dialogue and narration in Regency romance. Use this alongside the main SKILL.md to keep your prose historically grounded and readable.

---

## The Golden Rule

Period-accurate but READABLE. You are writing for modern readers who want to feel transported to the Regency, not modern readers who want to decode 19th-century prose. Lean toward Austen's clarity, not Fanny Burney's density.

---

## Contractions: The Non-Negotiable

**Never use contractions in Regency dialogue or narration.** This is the single fastest way to break the period voice.

| Never | Always |
|-------|--------|
| I'm | I am |
| don't | do not |
| can't | cannot |
| won't | shall not / will not |
| wouldn't | would not |
| couldn't | could not |
| shouldn't | ought not |
| wasn't | was not |
| weren't | were not |
| isn't | is not |
| aren't | are not |
| haven't | have not |
| hasn't | has not |
| it's | it is |
| that's | that is |
| there's | there is |
| he's / she's | he is / she is |
| they're | they are |
| we're | we are |
| you're | you are |
| let's | let us |
| who's | who is |
| what's | what is |

**Exception:** Servants or lower-class characters MAY occasionally use contractions to mark their social class in dialogue -- but use this sparingly and intentionally.

| Modern | Regency Options |

| will (1st person) | shall |
| while | whilst |
| among | amongst |
| try | endeavour |
| ask (formal) | enquire |
| matter (as verb) | signify ("It does not signify") |
| face (noun) | countenance |
| very / really | exceedingly, excessively, most, quite |
| a lot | a great deal |
| behavior | conduct, deportment |

| Modern | Regency Options |
|--------|----------------|

## Polite Expressions

These are the everyday currency of Regency conversation. Use them naturally and frequently.

### Apology and Pardon
- "I beg your pardon" (apology or requesting repetition)
- "Pray forgive me"
- "I must beg your indulgence"
- "I hope I have not given offence"

### Requests
- "Pray tell me"
- "If you would be so kind"
- "Might I trouble you to..."
- "Would you do me the honour of..."
- "I wonder if you would be so obliging as to..."

### Gratitude
- "I am much obliged to you"
- "You are too kind"
- "I thank you" (not "thank you" alone in formal settings)
- "I am exceedingly grateful"

### Hedging and Indirection
- "I am sure I do not know"
- "I cannot say"
- "It would not do"
- "That will never answer" (that will not work)
- "I am not at all certain that..."
- "One might venture to suggest..."
- "I dare say"
- "I fancy"
- "It is my understanding that..."

### Introductions and Greetings
- "How do you do?" (not "nice to meet you")
- "I am delighted to make your acquaintance"
- "Pray allow me to introduce..."
- "Good morning / Good day / Good evening"
- Farewell: "Good day" / "I bid you good day" / "Farewell" (NOT "goodbye" or "hello")

## Sentence Structure

### Regency prose favors:

**Inversion for emphasis:**
- "Never had she felt so mortified" (not "She had never felt so mortified")
- "Little did he suspect what awaited him"
- "Not for the world would she have confessed it"

**Dependent clauses first:**
- "Had she but known what awaited her, she might have remained at home"
- "Though she wished most heartily to decline, propriety demanded she accept"

**Indirect expression of strong feeling:**
- Characters rarely say "I love you" directly
- They speak of "regard," "esteem," "attachment," "affection," "tender feelings"
- "My feelings will not be repressed. You must allow me to tell you how ardently I admire and love you." (Darcy's confession is notable for being MORE direct than typical)

**Understatement:**
- "She was not displeased" = she was very happy
- "He was not entirely without merit" = he was quite impressive
- "She found his company not altogether disagreeable" = she was developing feelings

**Litotes (double negative for positive):**
- "It was not an unhappy arrangement" = it was a good arrangement
- "He was not unaware of her charms" = he was very aware

## Anachronisms: Words and Phrases to Avoid

### Words That Did Not Exist Yet

| Anachronism | Why | Use Instead |
|-------------|-----|-------------|
| OK / Okay | American, 1830s at earliest | Very well |
| Hello | Not common greeting until telephone era | Good day, good morning |
| Goodbye | Use was more limited | Good day, farewell, I bid you good day |
| Reliable | Not coined until later | Dependable (or rephrase: "a man one might trust") |
| Scientist | Coined 1834 by William Whewell | Natural philosopher |
| Photograph | Photography invented 1830s | Portrait, miniature, likeness |
| Train (transport) | Railways came later | Carriage, post, coach |
| Contact (as verb) | 20th century usage | Write to, call upon, send word to |
| Interesting (vague positive) | Not used this way in the period | Engaging, remarkable, of note |
| Bored | Modern casual usage | Suffering from ennui, finding tedium |
| Nice (meaning good) | Period meaning: precise, particular | Pleasant, agreeable, delightful |

### Words That Meant Something Different

| Word | Modern Meaning | Regency Meaning |
|------|---------------|-----------------|
| Nervous | Anxious | Having strong nerves (brave) |
| Nice | Good, pleasant | Precise, particular, fastidious |
| Fond | Loving | Could mean foolish |
| Sensible | Practical | Aware, perceptive ("sensible of your kindness") |
| Condescend | Be patronizing | To graciously lower oneself (could be positive) |
| Intercourse | Sexual relations | Conversation, social interaction |
| Ejaculate | Sexual | To exclaim suddenly |
| Gay | Homosexual | Merry, cheerful, bright |
| Awful | Bad | Awe-inspiring |
| Presently | At present | Soon |

## Physical and Material Anachronisms

### Things That Did Not Exist:
- Zippers (buttons, hooks, laces, pins)
- Elastic (drawstrings, ties)
- Synthetic fabrics (muslin, silk, cotton, wool, linen only)
- Modern undergarments (stays/corsets, chemises, drawers -- NOT bras or panties)
- Matches (tinderbox, or lighting a spill from an existing fire)
- Gas lighting in most homes (candles, oil lamps)
- Flush toilets in most homes (chamber pots, privies)
- Running water (servants carried water)
- Central heating (fireplaces only)

### Food and Drink Anachronisms:
- No tomato ketchup (tomatoes were suspicious)
- No bananas (extremely rare, expensive)
- Tea was expensive -- servants did not drink it casually
- Coffee houses were male spaces
- Chocolate was a morning drink, not a candy bar
- No carbonated beverages as we know them

## Dialogue Tags

Vary beyond "said" to maintain period flavor:

### Common Regency dialogue tags:
- observed, remarked, declared, pronounced
- enquired, demanded
- murmured, whispered
- exclaimed, cried
- replied, returned, responded, rejoined
- acknowledged, agreed, conceded
- protested, objected
- assured, averred
- ventured, suggested
- confessed, admitted

### Use "said" too:
"Said" is invisible and should still carry 50-60% of your dialogue tags. The period-specific tags are seasoning, not the main dish. Overusing "declared" and "exclaimed" becomes its own kind of anachronism -- a parody of the period rather than an evocation of it.

## Quick Self-Check

Before finalizing any Regency scene, scan for:

1. **Contractions** -- Search your text. Every one must go.
2. **"Should"** -- Replace with "ought" in most cases.
3. **"Will" in first person** -- Replace with "shall."
4. **"Hello" and "Goodbye"** -- Replace with period alternatives.
5. **"Okay" / "OK"** -- Replace with "very well."
6. **Modern slang or casual speech** -- If it sounds like it could be said in a coffee shop, it does not belong.
7. **Technology checks** -- No matches, gas lights (in early period), photographs, trains.
8. **Forms of address** -- Correct title for every character, every time.

---

*The goal is not to write a museum exhibit. The goal is to write prose that FEELS like the Regency -- that transports the reader without tripping them. Austen herself prioritized clarity. Follow her lead.*
