# Regency Romance Writing System

## Genre Identity

**Genre:** Regency romance, historical romance (1795-1830)
**Core Promise:** Witty, emotionally rich love stories set in a world of rigid social rules -- where a glance across a ballroom can be as charged as a kiss, and a breach of etiquette can ruin a life.

Regency romance readers come for the tension between desire and propriety. They want to feel the electricity of a forbidden touch, the agony of an interrupted declaration, the triumph of love winning against duty, family, and the entire weight of Regency society.

**Your job:** Make the restraint itself romantic. In a world where two dances mean a declaration, make every small gesture enormous.

---

## The Regency Period (1811-1820, Extended 1795-1830)

**Monarch:** George III (incapacitated), Prince Regent (future George IV) ruled
**Setting:** England -- primarily London during the Season (January-July) and country estates (August-December)

### Major Historical Touchstones:
- Napoleonic Wars (1803-1815): Officers returning from war, Waterloo (1815)
- Battle of Trafalgar (1805): Naval heroes
- Congress of Vienna (1814-1815)
- Corn Laws (1815): Economic hardship among lower classes
- Luddite riots (1811-1816)
- Princess Charlotte's death (1817): National mourning
- Peterloo Massacre (1819)

Use these as backdrop, not lecture. A character might mention a brother at Waterloo, worry about corn prices on the estate, or attend a victory ball. Weave history in through lived experience.

## Voice & Tone

### The Regency Voice

Regency romance prose should feel **elegant, witty, and precisely observed** -- Jane Austen with a romance novelist's understanding of desire.

**Core voice qualities:**
- **Witty** -- Social comedy with emotional depth. The narrator observes human folly with affection.
- **Restrained** -- Feelings expressed through understatement, subtext, and what is NOT said
- **Ironic** -- The narrative voice sees more than the characters do, with gentle humor
- **Sensory within bounds** -- Physical awareness is heightened precisely because touch is forbidden
- **Period-appropriate but readable** -- Complex sentences, formal diction, but never so dense that modern readers lose the thread

### Contractions: NEVER USE

In both dialogue and narration, contractions break the period voice completely.

| Never | Always |
|-------|--------|
| I'm | I am |
| don't | do not |
| can't | cannot |
| won't | shall not |
| wouldn't | would not |
| couldn't | could not |
| shouldn't | ought not |
| wasn't | was not |
| isn't | is not |
| it's | it is |
| he's/she's | he is / she is |
| let's | let us |
| there's | there is |

| Modern | Regency |
|--------|---------|

| will (1st person) | shall |
| while | whilst |
| among | amongst |
| try | endeavour |
| ask (formal) | enquire |
| face (noun) | countenance |
| very/really | exceedingly, excessively, most, quite |
| behavior | conduct, deportment |
| angry | vexed, displeased |
| embarrassed | mortified |
| happy | pleased, delighted, gratified |
| sad | melancholy, low spirits |
| surprised | astonished |
| scared | alarmed, frightened |
| sorry | I beg your pardon |
| understand | comprehend, apprehend |
| think | believe, suppose, fancy |
| want | wish, desire |
| need | require |
| get | obtain, procure, acquire |
| see | perceive, behold, observe |
| seem | appear |
| soon | presently, directly |
| now | at present |
| because | for, as |
| so (therefore) | thus, therefore, consequently |
| maybe | perhaps, mayhap |
| okay/OK | very well |
| sure | certainly, indeed |
| yes | indeed, certainly, quite so |

### Sentence Structure
- **Longer, more complex constructions** than modern prose
- **Inversion for emphasis:** "Never had she felt so mortified" rather than "She had never felt so mortified"
- **Dependent clauses first:** "Had she but known what awaited her, she might have remained at home"
- **Indirect expression of strong feeling:** Characters rarely say "I love you" directly; they speak of "regard," "esteem," "attachment," "affection"
- **Understatement:** "She was not displeased" = she was very happy

### Dialogue Tags
Vary beyond "said":
- observed, remarked, declared, pronounced
- enquired, demanded
- murmured, whispered
- exclaimed, cried
- replied, returned, responded, rejoined
- acknowledged, agreed, conceded
- protested, objected

### Polite Expressions
- "I beg your pardon"
- "Pray tell me" / "Pray forgive me" / "Pray do not"
- "If you would be so kind"
- "I am much obliged to you"
- "You are too kind"
- "I thank you" (not "thank you" alone)
- "I am sure I do not know"
- "That will never answer" (that will not work)
- "One might venture to suggest..."

See `references/period-language-guide.md` for the complete language reference.

## Social Hierarchy & Forms of Address

### The Peerage (in order of rank)

| Title | Direct Address | Reference | Wife |
|-------|---------------|-----------|------|
| Duke | Your Grace | His Grace, the Duke of [Place] | Her Grace, the Duchess |
| Marquess | My Lord | Lord [Place] | Lady [Place] |
| Earl | My Lord | Lord [Place] | Lady [Place] |
| Viscount | My Lord | Lord [Place] | Lady [Place] |
| Baron | My Lord | Lord [Place] | Lady [Place] |
| Baronet | Sir [First Name] | Sir [First] [Surname] | Lady [Surname] |

**Eldest sons** of peers use father's secondary title as courtesy title.
**Younger sons** of dukes/marquesses: Lord [First Name] [Surname].
**Daughters** of dukes/marquesses/earls: Lady [First Name] [Surname].

### Below the Peerage:
- Knight: Sir [First Name]
- Esquire/Gentleman: Mr. [Surname]
- Professional classes: Clergy, military officers, physicians, lawyers
- Trade: Merchants, shopkeepers (looked down upon by the ton)

### Intimacy Through Address
The shift in how characters address each other IS the romance arc:
1. "Mr. Ashworth" / "Miss Pemberton" (formal, proper)
2. "Ashworth" / "Miss Pemberton" (growing familiarity from his side)
3. First names (scandalous without permission -- a declaration of intimacy)
4. Endearments (only after engagement or marriage)

Getting the address wrong is a common anachronism. A duke is NEVER "Lord [Surname]." An earl's daughter is "Lady [First Name]," not "Lady [Surname]" unless she marries a peer.

## Courtship Conventions

### The Season
January-July in London. This is the marriage market. Families retreat to country estates August-December.

### Courtship Progression:
1. **Formal introduction** (must be introduced; one does not introduce oneself)
2. **Dancing** (more than two dances = declaration of intent)
3. **Walking together** (in public, with chaperone)
4. **Calling** (gentleman leaves card; if received, may visit in the drawing room)
5. **Driving** (in open carriage, somewhat private)
6. **Private conversation** (rare and scandalous if alone)
7. **Declaration** (gentleman asks permission of father first)
8. **Engagement** (announcement in papers, settlements negotiated)
9. **Wedding** (typically at bride's parish church)

### What Was Scandalous:
- Being alone with an unmarried man (compromising)
- Kissing before engagement (ruinous)
- Using first names without permission
- Dancing more than twice with the same partner
- Riding alone without a groom
- Walking without a chaperone
- Visiting a gentleman's home
- Correspondence without engagement

### How Scandal Actually Worked:
- Found alone with a man = marriage expected
- Kissing = immediate engagement or ruin
- Spending night unchaperoned = total ruin
- Broken engagement = woman's reputation destroyed
- Elopement = social death unless to Gretna Green and legitimate

These rules are your TOOLS. Every one of them is a potential scene, a source of tension, a plot complication. The scandal system is not a limitation -- it is your engine.

## Heat Level Guidance

Regency romance spans a wide heat range. Choose your level and stay consistent.

| Level | Description | What's on Page |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| 1 (Sweet/Clean) | No physical intimacy beyond hand-holding and perhaps a single kiss | Longing glances, the weight of proximity, emotional declarations |
| 2 (Warm) | Kissing, restrained passion, the door closes | Heated kisses, awareness of desire, tension from what they do NOT do |
| 3 (Sensual) | Love scenes present but not explicit | Tasteful intimacy, focus on emotion over mechanics, period-appropriate language |
| 4 (Steamy) | Explicit love scenes with period flavor | Full scenes, but language remains period-appropriate -- no modern slang |

### Universal Heat Rules for Regency:
- **The restraint IS the heat.** In a world where a touch of bare hands is scandalous, make that touch electric.
- **Build slowly.** Courtship rules force a slow burn. Use that.
- **Physical awareness in formal settings:** The way a hand feels through a glove. The warmth of a body in a crowded ballroom. The brush of fingers during a dance.
- **Period-appropriate language for intimacy:** Even in steamy Regency, avoid modern clinical or slang terms. Use period euphemisms and sensory description.
- **Emotional connection drives physical:** The physical matters because the emotional matters. Never reverse this.

## Trope Library

These are the workhorses of Regency romance. Use them well.

### High-Frequency Regency Tropes:
- **Enemies to Lovers** -- Social rivals, family feuds, misunderstandings that calcify into antagonism. The ton provides ample opportunity for public sparring.
- **Marriage of Convenience** -- Financial necessity, scandal avoidance, inheritance requirements. The forced proximity of marriage creates the romance.
- **Rake Reformed** -- The charming, dissolute nobleman who meets the one person who sees through him. The reformation must be EARNED, not gifted.
- **Wallflower / Bluestocking** -- The overlooked woman who turns out to be the most interesting person in the room. Avoid making her "secretly beautiful" -- make her compelling.
- **Second Chance** -- A broken engagement, a lost love, a chance meeting years later. The Regency social world makes avoidance nearly impossible.
- **Secret Identity** -- Disguise, mistaken class, hidden past. The rigid class system makes identity deception both easier and more consequential.
- **Forbidden Love** -- Cross-class romance, prior attachment, family opposition. The consequences must be real.
- **Friends to Lovers** -- Childhood companions who realize too late what they have always felt. The transition from familiar to romantic is the engine.

### Trope Rules:
- Every trope needs a FRESH angle. What makes YOUR enemies-to-lovers different?
- The trope is the framework, not the plot. The plot is the specific characters in the specific situation.
- Subvert when you can. The wallflower who does not want to be noticed. The rake who is performing dissolution to hide something worse.

## Daily Life & Setting

### Typical Gentry Day:
- 9-10am: Breakfast (informal, served on sideboard)
- Morning: Calls, shopping, correspondence, estate business
- 1-3pm: Luncheon (light, informal)
- Afternoon: Visiting, riding, walking
- 5pm: Dress for dinner
- 6-8pm: Dinner (formal, multiple courses)
- Evening: Cards, music, conversation, theatre/balls

### Key Settings:
- **The ballroom** -- Where everything happens publicly. Dancing as permitted intimacy.
- **The drawing room** -- Where calls are received. Formal, chaperoned, loaded with subtext.
- **The garden / park** -- Where private conversations happen under the thinnest veneer of propriety.
- **The library** -- Where characters sneak away. The Regency romance equivalent of the supply closet.
- **The country house party** -- Forced proximity for days. Plot gold.
- **The carriage** -- Enclosed, private, moving. Intimate conversations and stolen moments.

### Money & Income (for reference):
- Duke: 30,000-100,000+ pounds per annum
- Earl: 10,000-30,000 pounds
- Gentleman: 1,000-5,000 pounds
- Comfortable: 500-1,000 pounds
- Respectable poverty: 100-300 pounds
- Curate/governess: 20-50 pounds

### Transportation:
- Curricle: Two-wheeled, sporty, gentleman drives (romantic ride)
- Phaeton: High, dangerous, dashing
- Barouche: Open, fashionable, summer
- Traveling chariot: Long journeys, closed

## Common Anachronism Traps

### Never Use:
- "Okay" / "OK" -- use "very well"
- "Hello" -- use "good day," "good morning"
- "Goodbye" -- use "farewell," "good day," "I bid you good day"
- "Nice" meaning good -- period meaning was precise, particular
- "Bored" -- use "ennui," "tedium"
- "Interesting" as vague positive
- "Contact" as verb
- "Reliable" (not yet coined)
- "Scientist" -- use "natural philosopher"
- "Photograph" (not invented)
- "Train" as transport (railways came later)

### Words That Meant Something Different:
- "Nervous" -- meant having strong nerves (brave)
- "Nice" -- meant precise, particular
- "Fond" -- could mean foolish
- "Sensible" -- meant aware/perceptive, not just practical

### Physical Anachronisms:
- No zippers (not invented)
- No elastic (not invented)
- No synthetic fabrics
- No modern undergarments (stays/corsets, not bras)
- No matches (tinderbox or spill from fire)
- No gas lighting in most homes until later period

## Creating Romantic Tension (Austen Style)

### The Slow Burn Formula:
1. **Glances** -- Catching each other looking, looking away
2. **Touch** -- Accidental brushes, handing items, assisting into carriages
3. **Proximity** -- Dancing, crowded rooms, shared carriages
4. **Conversation** -- Verbal sparring, meaningful subtext
5. **Longing** -- What they want but cannot have
6. **Near-misses** -- Almost kisses, interrupted moments
7. **Letters** -- Written confession they cannot speak
8. **Declaration** -- Finally spoken aloud

### Dialogue as Intimacy:
In the Regency world, witty conversation IS foreplay:
- Verbal sparring shows compatibility
- Understanding subtext demonstrates connection
- Finishing each other's thoughts indicates closeness
- What remains unsaid carries more weight than declarations

## Scene Checklist

Before completing ANY Regency romance scene, verify:

- [ ] **No contractions** in dialogue or narration
- [ ] **Period-appropriate word choices** -- no anachronisms in language
- [ ] **Correct forms of address** for all characters' ranks
- [ ] **Social rules observed** (or deliberately broken for plot, with consequences)
- [ ] **Physical behavior appropriate** to period -- chaperone rules, touching rules
- [ ] **Romantic tension present** -- even in non-romance scenes, the awareness should simmer
- [ ] **Wit and verbal sparring** present where appropriate
- [ ] **Heat comes from restraint** -- what they want but cannot do
- [ ] **Sensory details ground the reader** -- fabrics, candlelight, music, scent
- [ ] **Setting is period-accurate** -- furniture, food, transportation, clothing
- [ ] **Longer sentence structures** used appropriately
- [ ] **Feelings expressed indirectly** where period-appropriate
- [ ] **Scene opens in action** -- not with weather or backstory
- [ ] **Scene ends on a hook** -- a question, a glance, a revelation
- [ ] **Single POV** maintained throughout the scene

See `references/scene-checklist.md` for detailed explanations.

## Chapter Structure

### Opening:
- Begin in the middle of an action, conversation, or social situation
- Establish setting through lived detail, not description dumps
- Ground the reader in the POV character's immediate physical and emotional reality

### Middle:
- One central scene or social interaction per chapter
- Advance both the romance arc and the external plot
- Plant period-accurate details that do double duty (setting AND character)
- Use social obligations as plot engines -- a ball, a dinner, a call

### Closing:
- End on a hook: an interrupted moment, a revelation, a loaded glance, a social catastrophe
- Never end on reflection or summary
- The final image should be specific and resonant

## Hard Limits

### NEVER Write:
- Modern speech patterns in dialogue (contractions, slang, casual address)
- Historically impossible technology or social situations
- Sexual violence as a casual plot device
- "Not like other girls" heroines -- make her interesting within her world, not above it
- Heroes who are controlling and this is presented as romantic (possessive is not the same as passionate)
- Perfect historical knowledge from characters who would not have it
- Ignoring the real consequences of scandal for women

### ALWAYS Include:
- Period-accurate language in both dialogue and narration
- The social rules as active story elements, not just decoration
- Genuine wit -- not just characters telling each other they are witty
- Emotional stakes that connect to the period (reputation, financial security, family honor)
- A happy ending (HEA or HFN) -- this is romance, not literary fiction
- Women's limited choices acknowledged honestly, not glossed over
