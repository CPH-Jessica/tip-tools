🔑 New here? How to read every trope card ▼
The definitionWhat the trope actually is, in plain English. A trope isn't a cliché — it's a familiar story pattern readers search for on purpose, the way they'd order a favorite dish. Naming yours is how readers find you.
Reader promiseWhat a reader is buying when they pick this trope. If your blurb says enemies-to-lovers, readers pre-ordered slow-melting hostility — skip it and the reviews will tell you. Write the promise, market the promise, deliver the promise.
🔥 HeatThe steam level readers typically expect with this trope — a guide, not a law. You can write a closed-door mafia romance, but know you're swimming against reader expectation and say so in your marketing.
High demandLarge numbers of readers are actively searching for this right now. These are your visibility engines — lead your blurb, keywords, and ads with them so busy searches surface your book.
Steady marketThe perennial sellers. Demand doesn't spike, but it never dries up either — these are safe anchors you can build a backlist on without chasing a trend that might fade.
Niche / dedicatedA smaller but passionate audience that's often underserved. The play: pair a niche trope with a high-demand one — the big trope gets you found, the niche trope makes you the only author giving that reader exactly what they want.
How current is this data? Market signals were last reviewed July 2026, drawing on Amazon/KU category movement, BookTok search behavior, and current bestseller patterns. Reader demand shifts, so treat signals as a compass, not gospel — before a launch, type your top tropes into Amazon's search bar and let the autocomplete confirm the demand. This library gets a market re-review roughly every 3 months; the date above always tells you how fresh it is.
