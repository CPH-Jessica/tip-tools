# Psychological Thriller Pacing Guide

## The Ratchet Principle

Tension in a psychological thriller is not a flat line or a constant peak. It is a **ratchet**: tighten, release slightly, tighten more. The floor keeps rising. The reader should never return to the comfort level of the opening chapter.

Think of it as breathing: inhale (tension builds), exhale (brief release), but each exhale is shallower than the last.

---

## Book-Level Pacing Structure

### Act 1: The Cage Established (Chapters 1-6, ~first 25%)

**Tension range: 3-5/10**

**Purpose:** Show the reader the cage before the protagonist sees it. Plant the wrongness. Establish the "normal" that is not normal.

| Chapter | Function | Tension |
|---------|----------|---------|
| 1 | Hook. Drop the reader into the protagonist's world with something unsettling -- not the main threat, but a signal that something is wrong. Establish voice, setting, and the protagonist's relationship to their environment. | 4 |
| 2 | Deepen the normal. Show the daily reality -- the routines, the relationships, the small accommodations. The reader should feel the weight of the life. | 3 |
| 3 | First crack. Something happens that does not fit the accepted version of reality. The protagonist notices but explains it away. | 4 |
| 4 | The control dynamic. Show the antagonist (person, system, or environment) in action. The reader should see the cage clearly now, even if the protagonist cannot. | 5 |
| 5 | The protagonist pushes back -- slightly. Tests a boundary. The system responds. | 5 |
| 6 | The inciting event. Something happens that makes the protagonist unable to look away. The cage becomes visible. This chapter should end on a strong hook that launches Act 2. | 6 |

**Act 1 rules:**
- Do NOT start at maximum tension. You have nowhere to go.
- Establish the protagonist's voice and physical reality in the first chapter.
- Plant at least 3 details that will pay off later in the book.
- The reader should feel uneasy by the end of chapter 2, even if they cannot say why.
- Every chapter still needs a hook. "Establishing" does not mean "boring."

---

### Act 2A: The Awakening (Chapters 7-12, ~25-50%)

**Tension range: 5-7/10**

**Purpose:** The protagonist begins to see. Each discovery tightens the trap. The cage responds to awareness.

| Chapter | Function | Tension |
|---------|----------|---------|
| 7 | Investigation begins. The protagonist starts looking -- at the space, at the relationship, at the system. What they find is worse than they expected. | 5 |
| 8 | An ally appears (or seems to). Someone who validates the protagonist's perception. But can they be trusted? | 5 |
| 9 | A revelation. The protagonist discovers something that reframes everything. A lie exposed, a room discovered, a pattern recognized. | 7 |
| 10 | The cage responds. The antagonist (or system) notices the protagonist's awakening and tightens control. Gaslighting escalates. Exits close. | 7 |
| 11 | The protagonist doubts themselves. Was the revelation real? Are they the problem? This is the psychological thriller's essential moment -- the self-doubt that keeps people trapped. | 6 |
| 12 | Midpoint turn. A discovery or event that makes returning to denial impossible. The protagonist commits to escape/confrontation. The tension floor permanently rises. | 7 |

**Act 2A rules:**
- Alternate between discovery chapters (protagonist gains information) and response chapters (the system pushes back).
- The protagonist should be wrong about at least one thing they think they have figured out.
- The ally introduced here should complicate things, not simplify them.
- Pacing should accelerate: chapters can get shorter as urgency builds.

---

### Act 2B: The Escalation (Chapters 13-18, ~50-75%)

**Tension range: 7-9/10**

**Purpose:** The cage fights back. Everything escalates. Allies disappear. The protagonist must choose: comply or fight. There is no middle ground left.

| Chapter | Function | Tension |
|---------|----------|---------|
| 13 | The cost. The protagonist pays a real price for their awakening. Something is taken, someone is lost, a safety net is removed. | 7 |
| 14 | Isolation. The protagonist's support structures fail or are revealed as part of the system. They are alone. | 8 |
| 15 | The false victory. The protagonist thinks they have found the way out. They have not. The trap is deeper than they knew. | 7 (rising to 8) |
| 16 | The dark night. The lowest point. The protagonist confronts the full scope of the cage. They see all the ways they participated in their own trapping. | 9 |
| 17 | The decision. From rock bottom, the protagonist chooses to fight. Not because they are strong, but because the alternative -- staying -- is finally worse than the risk. | 8 |
| 18 | The plan. The protagonist prepares for confrontation/escape. But the reader should feel the plan is fragile. This chapter launches Act 3. | 8 |

**Act 2B rules:**
- This section should feel suffocating. Chapters can be shorter. Paragraphs can be shorter. Breathing room disappears.
- The false victory in chapter 15 is essential -- it makes the real low point hit harder.
- The "dark night" chapter should be the most psychologically intense scene in the book.
- The decision to fight must come from character, not circumstance. The protagonist CHOOSES.

---

### Act 3: The Extraction (Chapters 19-24, ~75-100%)

**Tension range: 8-10/10**

**Purpose:** Confrontation and escape. Not rescue -- the protagonist saves themselves. The ending is won, not gifted.

| Chapter | Function | Tension |
|---------|----------|---------|
| 19 | Confrontation begins. The protagonist enacts their plan. Immediately, something goes wrong. | 9 |
| 20 | Adaptation. The protagonist must adjust in real time. Their understanding of the cage is tested. | 9 |
| 21 | The reveal. The final piece of truth. What the protagonist did not know -- about the antagonist, about themselves, about the cage. | 10 |
| 22 | The climax. The protagonist faces the antagonist/system/cage at full power. This is the scene the whole book has been building toward. | 10 |
| 23 | Extraction. The protagonist gets out -- but getting out is not clean. The cage leaves marks. The damage is real. | 8 |
| 24 | The new normal. Short. The protagonist in their after-life. Not healed, but free. Not whole, but choosing. The final image should resonate. | 6 |

**Act 3 rules:**
- The climax must require the protagonist to use self-knowledge gained throughout the book. Escape requires understanding, not just physical action.
- Do NOT wrap it up clean. The ending should feel won, not gifted. Survival IS the happy ending.
- The final chapter should be SHORT. Do not overexplain the aftermath.
- The last line of the book should be a concrete image, not a reflection.

## Chapter-Level Pacing

### Within each chapter:

**Opening (first 10%):**
- Start in the middle of something. Action, sensation, dialogue.
- Establish where the protagonist is and what is at stake.
- No backstory, no weather, no waking up.

**Rising action (10-70%):**
- The scene develops. Tension builds through action, dialogue, or discovery.
- Vary sentence length: longer during false calm, shorter as tension rises.
- At least one revelation or complication per chapter.

**Peak (70-90%):**
- The chapter's tension hits its highest point.
- A confrontation, a discovery, a moment of maximum pressure.

**Hook (final 10%):**
- Do NOT resolve the peak. Leave the reader hanging or shift to a new complication.
- End on a concrete image, question, or revelation.
- The "sleep test": could the reader put the book down here? If yes, rewrite the ending.

## Tension Variation Techniques

### To INCREASE tension:
- Shorter sentences
- Shorter paragraphs
- More white space on the page
- Sensory overload (too many sounds, too much detail)
- Cutting internal monologue -- the character stops thinking and starts reacting
- Physical symptoms: racing heart, shallow breath, tunnel vision
- Removing the protagonist's options one by one
- Silence after noise, or noise after silence

### To RELEASE tension (slightly):
- A moment of dark humor
- A brief memory that grounds the protagonist in who they were before the cage
- A physical comfort: a drink of water, a moment of warmth, a deep breath
- A scene of normalcy that the reader now sees differently
- A conversation with an ally (real or perceived)

### To build DREAD (slow tension):
- Longer sentences with dependent clauses that delay the point
- Ordinary details described with too much precision
- The protagonist noticing something is wrong but not naming it
- Repetition of a phrase or detail that accumulates meaning
- The absence of something expected

## Pacing Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Starting at Maximum Tension
If chapter 1 is a 9/10, you have nowhere to go. Start at 4 and build.

### 2. Flatline Tension
Every scene at the same level. The reader becomes numb. Vary the rhythm.

### 3. Tension Collapses
After a high point, the tension drops back to the opening level. The floor must keep rising.

### 4. Too Many "Breather" Scenes
One brief release after a peak is good. Three consecutive low-tension scenes and the reader puts the book down.

### 5. The Endless Act 2
Act 2 runs too long because the protagonist discovers, doubts, discovers, doubts in a loop. Each discovery must be NEW information that changes the situation.

### 6. The Rush to Resolution
Act 3 is too short. The climax happens too fast. The confrontation the reader has been waiting 200 pages for is over in 5. Give the climax room to breathe.

### 7. The Explanatory Ending
After the climax, 3 chapters of the protagonist explaining what happened and processing their feelings. One short chapter. Final image. Done.

## Word Count Guidelines

| Section | Chapters | Word Count | % of Novel |
|---------|----------|------------|------------|
| Act 1 | 6 | 15,000-20,000 | 20-25% |
| Act 2A | 6 | 18,000-22,000 | 22-27% |
| Act 2B | 6 | 18,000-22,000 | 22-27% |
| Act 3 | 6 | 15,000-20,000 | 20-25% |
| **Total** | **24** | **~75,000-85,000** | **100%** |

These are guidelines, not laws. Your story may need more or fewer chapters. But the proportional balance should hold: Acts 2A and 2B together should be roughly 50% of the book.

*The best psychological thrillers feel like a vice: the reader does not notice how tight it has gotten until they cannot breathe.*
